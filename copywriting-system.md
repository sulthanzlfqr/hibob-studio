# Hibob Studio — Copywriting System

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 2026-05-30  
**Source of Truth:** positioning.md + information-architecture-v2.md + homepage-blueprint-v2.md  
**Status:** Writing Source of Truth — applies to website, dashboard, products, pricing, marketing, onboarding

> This document defines how Hibob Studio writes. Not what it says — that is defined in the positioning and blueprint documents. This document defines how it says it. Every word written for Hibob Studio — on the website, in the panel, in onboarding, in error messages — should be written in accordance with this system.

---

## Section 1 — Voice & Tone

### Voice

Voice is who Hibob Studio is in writing. It does not change based on context.

Hibob Studio's voice is: **technical without being intimidating, direct without being cold, confident without being arrogant, empathetic without being soft.**

The voice comes from a specific kind of person: someone who has lived the problems the platform solves, built the solutions themselves, and now explains those solutions to others who are in the same situation they were once in. This is not the voice of a marketing team. It is the voice of a builder who is also a communicator.

**The voice in one sentence:** Hibob Studio writes like a senior Roblox developer who also understands how to run a product — and who respects the intelligence of the creators they're building for.

---

### Tone

Tone is how Hibob Studio speaks in a specific context. It adjusts. The voice does not.

| Context | Tone |
|---------|------|
| Homepage hero | Declarative, confident |
| Problem section | Empathetic, specific |
| Product description | Functional, direct |
| Onboarding | Guiding, clear |
| Error messages | Calm, helpful, never apologetic beyond what's warranted |
| Success states | Warm, brief |
| Pricing | Transparent, matter-of-fact |
| Community (Discord) | Casual, builder-to-builder |

The tone shifts, but the underlying personality — builder, direct, knowledgeable, respectful — does not.

---

### Communication Style

**Short sentences.** Complex ideas explained simply. Not simplified ideas explained complexly. There is a difference.

**Active voice.** "Audio Forge publishes your tracks" not "Your tracks are published by Audio Forge." The platform does things. The platform is active. The writing reflects this.

**Specific over general.** "Upload 30 audio tracks in one session" not "Streamline your audio workflow." The specific claim is more credible and more useful than the vague one.

**Problem-first.** When introducing a product or feature, name the problem before naming the solution. The creator's problem is what they already know and feel. The solution is what they need to learn. Give them the familiar before the new.

**No throat-clearing.** Every sentence does work. "Hibob Studio is proud to introduce..." does not do work. "Audio Forge publishes your Roblox audio in batches." does work.

---

### Writing Principles

1. Write for the creator who is reading, not for the writer who is writing.
2. Specificity is credibility. Vagueness is a signal that there is nothing specific to say.
3. If you need a jargon word, earn it by defining it once, clearly.
4. Every sentence should either inform, persuade, or guide. Not all three at once.
5. Read every sentence aloud. If it sounds like marketing, rewrite it. If it sounds like a person talking to another person about a real problem, keep it.

---

### We sound like:

- A developer explaining a tool to another developer — knowledgeable, specific, no condescension
- A builder who respects the reader's intelligence enough to get to the point
- Someone who has experienced the problem they are describing
- A product that takes itself seriously without taking itself too seriously
- Bilingual (ID/EN) without losing tone in either language

---

### We do not sound like:

- A startup trying to sound like a startup ("disrupting the creator economy")
- A freelancer selling themselves ("I'm passionate about building exceptional Roblox experiences")
- A corporate brand ("We are committed to empowering creators through innovative solutions")
- A generic SaaS ("The #1 platform for creator success")
- An AI-generated product description ("Unleash the power of your creative potential")
- A hype machine ("This changes everything")
- A friend who overclaims ("trust me, this is insane")

## Section 2 — Brand Personality in Writing

### How Hibob Studio speaks

Hibob Studio speaks like a technical peer — not like someone explaining down to the creator, and not like someone performing excitement to get a sale. The assumed baseline of the reader is someone who knows what Luau scripting is, who has dealt with Roblox moderation queues, and who has spent hours doing something that should have taken minutes. Write for that person.

Short clauses. Declarative statements. When a sentence is getting long, it should be because the information requires it — not because more words were added for weight.

**Examples:**

Too long, trying too hard:
> "At Hibob Studio, we deeply understand the unique challenges that serious Roblox creators face on a daily basis, which is why we've developed a comprehensive suite of interconnected tools designed specifically to address those pain points."

Correct:
> "Serious Roblox creators have real operational problems. Hibob Studio builds the infrastructure that solves them."

---

### How Hibob Studio explains complexity

Hibob Studio's products involve technical architecture — Creator Identity linking Discord and Roblox, License Manager automating whitelist pipelines, API Infrastructure enabling third-party integration. These are not simple concepts. But the complexity is never the point.

**Rule:** Explain what changes for the creator, not how the system works.

The creator does not need to understand how Creator Identity stores linked accounts. They need to understand that their buyers' Discord and Roblox identities are verified automatically so they never have to check manually again.

The mechanism is for documentation. The consequence is for copy.

**Structure for explaining complex features:**
1. State the problem (what the creator currently does manually)
2. State what changes (what the creator no longer has to do)
3. State the mechanism only if it adds credibility or is required for setup context

**Example — explaining Creator Identity:**

Too technical:
> "Creator Identity uses OAuth 2.0 linked account verification across Discord and Roblox APIs to create a unified identity graph."

Too vague:
> "Connect your accounts for a seamless experience."

Correct:
> "Creator Identity links your Discord account to your Roblox profile. Once connected, the platform knows who your buyers, donors, and community members are — in both places — without you having to verify anyone manually."

---

### How Hibob Studio sells

Hibob Studio does not sell by persuading. It sells by demonstrating.

The difference: persuasion argues that the product is good. Demonstration shows what the product changes. Creators evaluate products by imagining themselves using them. Copy that helps them imagine the workflow change is more effective than copy that argues the product's quality.

**Sell by showing the before and the after.**

Not: "Audio Forge is a powerful audio publishing tool for serious Roblox creators."

But: "Before Audio Forge: upload track 1. Wait. Copy the asset ID. Upload track 2. Wait. Copy the asset ID. After Audio Forge: drop your folder. Done."

The before/after is the sales argument. The product features are the evidence.

---

### How Hibob Studio builds trust

Trust is built by being specific, being accurate, and not overclaiming.

Every claim that can be backed with a number should be backed with a number. Every testimonial should be attributable. Every product described should actually exist (Coming Soon products are shown as Coming Soon — not implied to be available).

**Trust through specificity:** "30+ creators using platform" is specific. "Thousands of creators" when the number is 30 is a lie, and creators will know it.

**Trust through restraint:** Not claiming things the platform cannot yet deliver. Not using superlatives that cannot be proven. Not comparing favorably to competitors by name unless the comparison is specific and fair.

**Trust through acknowledgment:** When a product is in development, say so. When a feature has a limitation, state it. Creators who find an undisclosed limitation become distrustful. Creators who were told about a limitation and chose to proceed remain trusting.

---

### How Hibob Studio introduces products

Every product introduction follows the same structure — regardless of product complexity:

1. **Name the problem** — the specific, daily friction the product eliminates
2. **State what changes** — what the creator's workflow looks like after adoption
3. **Name the mechanism briefly** — only enough to answer "how?"
4. **Show the outcome** — what is now possible that was not possible before

This structure ensures that product introductions are written for the reader, not for the product team. The reader cares about what changes. The product team cares about how it works. The copy serves the reader.

## Section 3 — Messaging Pillars

The five messaging pillars are the load-bearing structures of all Hibob Studio communication. Every piece of copy — from a headline to an error message — is anchored to at least one of these pillars. When a piece of copy cannot be anchored to any pillar, it should be questioned.

---

### Pillar 1 — Infrastructure, Not Services

**Name:** Infrastructure  
**Purpose:** Define the category. Establish what Hibob Studio is and is not.  
**Meaning:** Hibob Studio does not do work for creators. It builds the systems that allow creators to do their work more efficiently and professionally. This distinction is fundamental. Services are consumed. Infrastructure is adopted.  

**Example usage:**
- Homepage hero: "Creator infrastructure for serious Roblox developers."
- About page: "We build the systems. You build the games."
- Panel onboarding: "Your creator infrastructure is ready. Connect your identity to begin."
- Error copy: Should never suggest contacting Hibob Studio to do something for the creator — always guide the creator to resolve it themselves using the platform.

**Signs a piece of copy violates this pillar:**
- Implies that Hibob Studio will do something for the creator
- Uses language of services ("we handle," "we manage," "we take care of")
- Positions Hibob Studio as a contractor or team member

---

### Pillar 2 — Connected, Not Collected

**Name:** Ecosystem  
**Purpose:** Communicate that products work together, not independently.  
**Meaning:** The value of Hibob Studio is not any single product — it is the way the products connect. Creator Identity enables License Manager. License Manager data is visible in Creator Panel. Audio Forge output feeds into Asset Manager. When copy treats products as independent tools, the platform's core value proposition is lost.  

**Example usage:**
- Ecosystem section headline: "Eight tools. One system."
- Product page: "Works with Creator Identity — buyers are verified automatically when they receive a license."
- Plans description: "Access the full ecosystem from one subscription."
- Panel sidebar labels: Products listed as a connected suite, not a menu

**Signs a piece of copy violates this pillar:**
- Presents a product as standalone ("buy Audio Forge separately")
- Does not reference how a product connects to the rest of the platform
- Describes products in a list without communicating their relationships

---

### Pillar 3 — Time Reclaimed

**Name:** Time  
**Purpose:** Make the value of the platform concrete. Time is the universal currency.  
**Meaning:** Every product feature ultimately translates to time: time saved, time redirected from overhead to creation. This pillar makes abstract platform benefits concrete. "Automated whitelist" is abstract. "You will never manually add a buyer to a whitelist again" is concrete. The time implication is explicit.  

**Example usage:**
- Audio Forge: "Stop spending Sunday uploading audio. 30 tracks in 10 minutes."
- License Manager: "Your buyer gets access the moment they pay. No manual step."
- Donation Platform: "Reward your donors automatically. You won't see the transaction."
- Workflow section: "Monday used to start with two hours of administrative overhead. Now it starts with building."

**Signs a piece of copy violates this pillar:**
- Describes features without connecting them to time impact
- Uses efficiency language without being specific ("streamlined," "optimized")
- Focuses on feature capabilities rather than what the creator stops doing

---

### Pillar 4 — Professional Operation

**Name:** Professional  
**Purpose:** Establish the outcome Hibob Studio enables — not just efficiency, but operational credibility.  
**Meaning:** Creators who use Hibob Studio operate like professional studios, not like individual hobbyists who happen to have a following. The platform raises the operational floor of what "serious creator" looks like. This is aspirational but grounded — it describes the actual outcome of adoption, not a vague promise.  

**Example usage:**
- Homepage subheadline: "Built for creators who run their presence like a professional."
- License Manager: "Protect your products. Automate your distribution. Operate like a studio."
- Creator Plans: "The plan for creators who take their work seriously."
- About page: "We built this because serious creators deserve professional infrastructure."

**Signs a piece of copy violates this pillar:**
- Targets beginners or casual creators (this platform is for serious creators)
- Makes the platform sound like a hobby tool
- Underestimates the sophistication of the primary audience

---

### Pillar 5 — Built for Roblox

**Name:** Specificity  
**Purpose:** Differentiate from generic SaaS. Establish that Hibob Studio was built by and for the Roblox creator community — not adapted from somewhere else.  
**Meaning:** The specificity of Hibob Studio's Roblox focus is its competitive advantage over generic tools. Google Drive does not understand Roblox asset IDs. Sociabuzz does not know what a Roblox group is. Hibob Studio does. This Roblox-native depth must be audible in the writing.  

**Example usage:**
- Category claim: "The only creator infrastructure built specifically for Roblox."
- Audio Forge: "Audio pipeline built for how Roblox publishing actually works."
- Creator Identity: "Linked identity across Discord and Roblox — not just one or the other."
- Donation Platform: "In-game reward integration that Roblox understands."

**Signs a piece of copy violates this pillar:**
- Could describe a tool for any creator, not specifically a Roblox creator
- Uses generic SaaS language that doesn't reference Roblox context
- Obscures the Roblox-specific value in favor of broader appeal

## Section 4 — Core Vocabulary

These are the words Hibob Studio prefers and the words it uses consistently. Consistency in vocabulary creates a distinct brand voice — when the same word is used across every surface, the reader begins to associate that word with the brand.

---

### Preferred vocabulary

| Word | Use it because | Instead of |
|------|---------------|-----------|
| **Creator** | Specific to the audience; implies creative agency and ownership | User, customer, client, player, developer (unless referring to technical role specifically) |
| **Workflow** | Describes the daily operational reality of a creator — specific and recognizable | Process, system, procedure, methodology |
| **Infrastructure** | Names the category; distinguishes from "tool" or "service" | Tool, service, software, platform (when describing the category specifically) |
| **Publish / Publishing** | Roblox-specific action; more precise than "upload" in this context | Upload, submit, post, push |
| **Pipeline** | Technical but understood by the audience; implies automated sequence | Steps, process, method |
| **Connected** | Describes the ecosystem relationship; implies design, not coincidence | Integrated, linked, synced |
| **Automate / Automated** | The core value of infrastructure — removal of manual overhead | Automatic, smart, intelligent, streamlined |
| **Identity** | Specific to Creator Identity product and to the concept of verified presence | Account, profile, login |
| **Ecosystem** | Describes the eight products as one connected system | Suite, collection, bundle, set of tools |
| **Operate / Operations** | Frames creator work as professional — running something, not just making something | Manage, handle, deal with |
| **Access** | Frames subscription as gaining entry to infrastructure — not buying a product | Purchase, buy, get, subscribe |
| **Session** | Refers to time in the Creator Panel — implies active use, not passive browsing | Visit, use, login |
| **Protect** | Specific to License Manager — emotional resonance; creator's work has value worth protecting | License, whitelist, restrict |
| **Forge** | Product-specific vocabulary in "Audio Forge" — implies active transformation, not passive hosting | Process, handle, manage |
| **Verify / Verified** | Creator Identity's core action — specific and trust-implying | Check, confirm, validate |
| **Layer** | Describes the ecosystem architecture — foundation, content, monetization layers | Level, tier (when used for architecture) |

---

### Why vocabulary consistency matters

When a creator reads "Creator Identity" in the onboarding flow, they should be hearing the same word they read on the homepage. When they read "automate" in a product description, it should carry the same meaning it carried in the problem section. Vocabulary drift — using synonyms randomly — makes the brand feel inconsistent and the writing feel AI-generated.

Maintain a vocabulary and enforce it. When in doubt, check this document.

## Section 5 — Words to Avoid

These words are banned from Hibob Studio copy. Not discouraged — banned. Every occurrence is a copy failure.

---

### Banned: Superlatives without evidence

| Banned word | Why | Replace with |
|------------|-----|-------------|
| **Revolutionary** | Implies the platform changes the category — a claim that requires extraordinary evidence. Cannot be self-applied. | Nothing. Make the specific claim that would justify the word. |
| **Best** | Self-awarded. No one who reads "best" believes it. | Specific comparison with evidence, or remove the comparison entirely. |
| **Ultimate** | Implies finality. No platform is the final version of itself. | Specific feature or outcome language. |
| **Game-changing** | Ironic use of this word in a Roblox context aside — it's empty. | Show what changes. Don't describe the change. |
| **Next-generation** | Temporal marketing claim. No meaning. | Describe what is technically different, specifically. |
| **World-class** | Self-awarded, regional audience. Incongruent. | Nothing. Earn credibility through product quality. |
| **Powerful** | Empty modifier. Every product claims to be "powerful." | What does the product do that is powerful? Say that instead. |
| **Robust** | SaaS word. Sounds like marketing, not product knowledge. | Describe the capability specifically. |
| **Seamless** | The most overused word in SaaS copy. Every experience claims to be seamless. | Describe why the transition is frictionless, specifically. |
| **Intuitive** | Self-awarded UX claim. Show the interface, don't claim the quality. | Remove entirely, or show the interface. |
| **Innovative** | Means nothing without context. All companies claim to be innovative. | Describe what is new about the approach, specifically. |

---

### Banned: Agency and freelancer language

| Banned word/phrase | Why | Replace with |
|-------------------|-----|-------------|
| **"We'll handle it"** | Implies service relationship — Hibob Studio is not handling anything for the creator. | "The platform automates [specific thing]." |
| **"Let us know"** | Implies creator needs to contact Hibob Studio to get something done. | "Open a support ticket" or "Check our docs." |
| **"Commission"** | The retired business model. Zero occurrences allowed. | "Subscription," "access," "plan" |
| **"Hire"** | Zero occurrences. No form of "hire." | Depends on context — but if "hire" is needed, the copy is wrong. |
| **"Client"** | Implies service relationship. Hibob Studio has creators, not clients. | "Creator," "user" |
| **"Project"** | Implies commission relationship ("your project"). | "Your game," "your community," "your workflow" |
| **"Deliver"** | "We deliver results." Agency language. | Describe what the platform does specifically. |

---

### Banned: Corporate language

| Banned word/phrase | Why |
|-------------------|-----|
| **"Empower"** | Overused to the point of meaninglessness. |
| **"Solution"** | "Our solution addresses..." — corporate, impersonal, empty. |
| **"Leverage"** | Business jargon. |
| **"Synergy"** | Never acceptable. |
| **"Value-add"** | Jargon. |
| **"Best-in-class"** | Self-awarded comparative claim. |
| **"Cutting-edge"** | Temporal marketing claim. No meaning. |
| **"State-of-the-art"** | Same. |
| **"Comprehensive solution"** | Double jargon. |

---

### Banned: AI-generated tells

These phrases appear frequently in AI-generated copy and signal to readers that the copy was not written by someone who has thought about it:

- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "Whether you're a [A] or a [B]..."
- "Designed with you in mind"
- "Take your [X] to the next level"
- "The future of [category]"
- "Unlock the power of..."
- "Your journey begins here"
- "We understand your needs"
- Any sentence that ends with "so you can focus on what matters"

## Section 6 — Headline System

Headlines do one job: make the reader want to read what comes next. Nothing else. A headline that is clever but does not communicate is a failed headline. A headline that communicates but is not clever is a successful headline.

---

### Homepage hero headline

**Job:** Declare the category in 4-8 words.

**Rules:**
- Starts with the platform or its category — not with "we," "I," or a verb
- Names the audience or the category or both
- Does not contain a question (questions in headlines are passive; declarations are active)
- Does not contain "the" as the first word (weak opener)
- Contains no superlatives, no comparatives, no claims that require evidence

**Structure options:**
- [Category] for [Audience]: "Creator infrastructure for serious Roblox developers."
- [Audience] + [What changes]: "Roblox creators who operate professionally use Hibob Studio."
- [Declarative claim about the platform]: "The operating layer for serious Roblox creators."

**Not:**
- "We Build Tools For Roblox Creators" — "we" first, service language ("build tools")
- "The Best Platform for Roblox Development" — "best" banned, "platform for development" vague
- "Are You a Serious Roblox Creator?" — question, passive

---

### Section headlines (H2)

**Job:** Tell the reader what this section is about before they decide to read it.

**Rules:**
- One sentence or fragment, max 8 words
- States the subject of the section, not the outcome of reading it
- Does not start with "Discover," "Explore," "Learn," "Find out" — these are passive invitations, not section declarations
- Can be a declarative fragment: "The problem with manual audio publishing."
- Can be a direct statement: "Eight products. One system."
- Should not be a question unless the section is literally structured as a Q&A

**Examples — correct:**

| Section | Headline |
|---------|----------|
| Problem section | "Your workflow is costing you hours you don't notice." |
| Ecosystem section | "One platform. Eight connected tools." |
| Creator Identity | "Everything connects through your identity." |
| Social proof | "Creators who've made the switch." |
| Plans | "Choose your level of access." |

**Examples — incorrect:**

| Incorrect headline | Why |
|-------------------|-----|
| "Discover Our Amazing Platform Ecosystem" | "Discover," "amazing" — both banned |
| "Are You Ready to Level Up?" | Question, "level up" — banned |
| "Why Hibob Studio is the Best Choice" | "best" — banned |

---

### Product headlines

**Job:** Name the product's most important workflow change in one line.

**Rules:**
- Opens with the problem or the outcome — not the product name
- The product name can appear as a sub-label, but the headline itself is about the creator's experience
- Avoids feature language (what the product has); uses consequence language (what the creator gets)
- Length: 6-12 words

**Structure:** [Before state] → [After state] or [What you stop doing] or [What becomes possible]

**Examples:**

Audio Forge:
> "Stop uploading audio one track at a time."

License Manager:
> "Your buyers get access the moment they pay."

Donation Platform:
> "Community donations that know who your donors are."

Asset Manager:
> "All your assets. One place. Always current."

Creator Panel:
> "Everything you operate. One view."

---

### Pricing headlines

**Job:** Make the pricing section feel like an access decision, not a cost decision.

**Rules:**
- Frames the plan as a level of access or capability, not a price point
- Does not lead with the number ("From Rp X per month")
- Does not use words like "affordable," "cheap," "budget-friendly" — these frame price as the primary concern
- Uses the tier name as the headline, with the key differentiator as the subhead

**Examples:**

| Tier | Headline | Subhead |
|------|----------|---------|
| Free | "Start building." | "Full platform access. No payment required to begin." |
| Creator | "Operate professionally." | "Everything serious creators need to run their presence." |
| Studio | "Run a studio." | "Multi-user access, expanded capacity, priority support." |

---

### CTA headlines (in closing CTA sections)

**Job:** Invite a specific action without selling or pressuring.

**Rules:**
- One sentence, declarative
- Names what is available, not what the creator should feel
- Does not use "now" as a pressure word ("Start now!" — avoid)
- Does not end with an exclamation point in the headline
- Should feel like the conclusion of the page's argument, not a last-ditch push

**Examples:**

Correct:
> "Your creator infrastructure is ready."
> "Everything in one place. Open your panel."
> "The platform is built. Access it."

Incorrect:
> "Don't miss out — start today!"
> "Ready to transform your Roblox presence?"
> "Join thousands of creators!"

## Section 7 — Subheadline System

Subheadlines (the supporting paragraph beneath a headline) are where the headline's claim is grounded in specificity. The headline declares. The subheadline proves or elaborates — with constraint.

---

### Length rules

**Hero subheadline:** Maximum 2 sentences. Ideally 1. It should be readable in one breath.

**Section subheadlines:** Maximum 2-3 sentences. If a section subheadline exceeds 3 sentences, it is either doing the work of body copy (wrong) or the headline is not carrying enough weight (also wrong).

**Product subheadlines:** Maximum 2 sentences. After 2 sentences, you are in product description territory.

---

### Structure rules

**Sentence 1:** Concretize the headline's claim. If the headline says "One platform. Eight connected tools." — sentence 1 might say: "Audio, assets, identity, licensing, and community — all managed from one Creator Panel."

**Sentence 2 (if needed):** Bridge to action or set up the section content. "Here's how each piece connects."

**Do not:**
- Use the subheadline to introduce new concepts not mentioned in the headline
- Use the subheadline to make claims that should be in the headline
- Repeat the headline in different words (this is the most common subheadline failure)
- Begin with "We" — the reader's experience is the subject, not Hibob Studio's actions

---

### Information density rules

Subheadlines carry medium information density. They are not body copy — they should not attempt to explain everything. They are not headlines — they should not be declarative fragments.

The subheadline's job is to give the reader enough specificity to decide whether to read the body copy below. It is a preview, not a summary.

**Test:** If a reader reads only the headline and subheadline, do they know (a) what this section is about, and (b) whether it is relevant to them? If yes, the subheadline is working.

---

### Messaging hierarchy in subheadlines

Subheadlines follow from their parent headline in messaging level:

- If the headline is at Pillar level (Infrastructure, Ecosystem), the subheadline should descend to Platform level (specific platform behavior)
- If the headline is at Platform level, the subheadline should descend to Product level (specific product outcome)
- If the headline is at Product level, the subheadline should descend to Feature level (specific capability or workflow change)

Never ascend in the subheadline — never write a subheadline that is more abstract than its headline.

## Section 8 — CTA System

### CTA philosophy

A CTA is a directional signal, not a persuasion device. By the time a reader encounters a CTA, the surrounding copy has done the persuasion. The CTA's job is to name the next step with enough specificity that the reader knows exactly what happens when they click.

"Click here" is the worst CTA ever written because "here" describes nothing. "Get started" is almost as bad because "started" at what? The best CTA label is the one that a creator reads and thinks: "yes, that is exactly what I want to do next."

---

### CTA rules

**Rule 1 — Describe the result, not the attitude.**

The CTA should name what happens after the click, not how the creator should feel about clicking.

| Attitude CTA (avoid) | Result CTA (use) |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| "Get started today!" | "Open Creator Panel" |
| "Transform your workflow!" | "Try Audio Forge" |
| "Join the revolution!" | "Join the community" |
| "Take the leap!" | "Start free" |

**Rule 2 — Be specific about the destination.**

Generic destination: "Learn more" — where? About what?  
Specific destination: "See the Audio Forge workflow" — clear, specific, honest.

**Rule 3 — Match verb to action.**

| Action type | Verb |
|------------|------|
| Opening the panel | "Open" |
| Starting a process | "Start" |
| Accessing a specific product | "Try [Product Name]" |
| Viewing more content | "See" or "Explore" |
| Subscribing | "Start [Plan Name]" |
| Connecting accounts | "Connect" |

**Rule 4 — Primary CTA is active; secondary CTA is exploratory.**

Primary CTAs use action verbs: Open, Start, Try, Connect.  
Secondary CTAs use exploration verbs: See, Explore, Learn, Compare.

**Rule 5 — No exclamation points on CTAs.** An exclamation point on a CTA communicates enthusiasm about the platform, not confidence in the product.

---

### Good CTA / Bad CTA examples

| Context | Bad CTA | Good CTA |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Hero primary | "Hire Me" | "Open Creator Panel" |
| Hero secondary | "Click to learn more" | "See the ecosystem" |
| Audio Forge spotlight | "Get Audio Forge Now!" | "Try Audio Forge" |
| License Manager spotlight | "Buy License Manager" | "Protect your products" |
| Closing CTA | "Don't miss out — start today" | "Access the platform" |
| Plan tier | "Choose This Plan" | "Start Creator" |
| Community section | "Follow us!" | "Join the community" |
| Documentation | "Check it out" | "Read the API docs" |

## Section 9 — Product Copy System

### How products are introduced

Every product introduction on every surface — homepage, product page, onboarding, panel — follows a consistent structure. The structure is not a template to be filled in mechanically. It is an order of operations: problem → change → mechanism → outcome.

**Product introduction order of operations:**

1. **The problem** — what the creator currently does manually or what currently doesn't work
2. **The change** — what is different after adopting this product (workflow-level, not feature-level)
3. **The mechanism** — briefly, how the product makes the change happen (only as much as needed to make the change believable)
4. **The outcome** — what is now possible, or what is now gone, that matters to the creator

This is not always four sentences. Sometimes the problem and change can be merged. Sometimes the mechanism is obvious and doesn't need stating. But the order is always the same.

---

### Audio Forge — copy rules

**Core problem:** Roblox audio publishing is manual, time-consuming, and does not scale. One track takes 4 minutes. A 30-track update takes 2 hours.

**The change:** Batch processing. A folder of tracks becomes a set of published asset IDs without manual intervention.

**Copy rules for Audio Forge:**
- Always reference the specific friction: uploading one track at a time
- Always reference the time contrast: hours vs. minutes
- Do not describe audio processing in technical terms unless in documentation
- The word "Forge" in the product name implies transformation — lean into this: "Forge your audio pipeline" is on-voice
- Never describe it as an "uploader" — it is a pipeline, which implies automation and sequence

**Short description (product card level):**
> "Batch audio publishing for Roblox. Upload a folder. Get asset IDs. Done."

**Medium description (product page level):**
> "Audio Forge replaces the manual upload cycle that costs Roblox creators hours every week. Drop your audio folder. Forge processes each track, handles the Roblox moderation queue, and returns a complete set of asset IDs — ready for your game. What used to take a Sunday afternoon now takes ten minutes."

**What to avoid:**
- "Powerful audio tool" — "powerful" banned
- "Seamlessly integrates" — "seamlessly" banned
- "Upload your music easily" — "easily" is passive and vague

---

### Donation Platform — copy rules

**Core problem:** Donation platforms that exist (Sociabuzz, Saweria) don't know who the donor is in Roblox. Rewards have to be issued manually. The connection between "someone donated" and "they get something in the game" requires human action.

**The change:** The platform knows who the donor is in Discord and Roblox simultaneously (via Creator Identity). Reward happens automatically upon donation. No human step between payment and reward.

**Copy rules for Donation Platform:**
- Always tie the product to Creator Identity — the connection is the differentiator
- Emphasize the automation: "reward issued automatically" not "easy reward management"
- Reference the community relationship: donors are part of the creator's community, not anonymous transactions
- Use "community support" as the framing, not "revenue" — the emotional resonance for club owners is community appreciation, not monetization

**Short description:**
> "Community donations that know who your donors are. In-game rewards issued automatically."

**Medium description:**
> "Donation Platform connects your community's support to your game. When a donor contributes, Creator Identity verifies who they are — in Discord and in Roblox. Their in-game reward is issued without any action from you. The transaction is complete before you've seen the notification."

**What to avoid:**
- "Monetize your audience" — transactional framing that reduces community to revenue
- "Accept donations easily" — "easily" banned
- Describing it as an alternative to Sociabuzz by name (makes it sound reactive rather than original)

---

### License Manager — copy rules

**Core problem:** Developer who sells systems manually manages their buyers: verify identity, add to whitelist, confirm, track. When a buyer shares the product, there's no enforcement. When a license expires (if it does), there's no system.

**The change:** Payment → license issued → access granted → automated. No manual step between purchase and access. License records tracked in the platform.

**Copy rules for License Manager:**
- Emphasize automation specifically: "the moment they pay" is the key phrase
- Use "protect" language: creators protect their work, not just "manage licenses"
- Acknowledge the piracy reality without dwelling on it
- Reference the connection to Creator Identity: license is issued to an identity, not just a Roblox username

**Short description:**
> "Protect your work. Automate your buyers. License Manager issues access the moment payment is confirmed."

**Medium description:**
> "License Manager ends the manual whitelist cycle. When a buyer completes their purchase, License Manager verifies their creator identity and issues their license automatically. No Discord DMs. No manual whitelist additions. No following up. Your work is protected. Your buyers are served. You weren't in the room for any of it."

**What to avoid:**
- "DRM for Roblox" — technical language that may trigger defensive reactions
- "Never worry about piracy again" — overclaim; piracy still exists, License Manager reduces it
- "Powerful license management tool" — "powerful" banned

---

### Asset Manager — copy rules

**Core problem:** Creator assets (models, scripts, sounds, images) are scattered across Roblox toolbox, Google Drive, local machine, Discord DMs. Finding a specific asset from six months ago requires searching multiple locations. Versioning doesn't exist.

**The change:** One repository. Everything searchable. Versions tracked. Deployment from one place.

**Copy rules for Asset Manager:**
- The primary pain point is scatter — emphasize this before the solution
- "Versioned" is a technical term that this audience understands — use it
- Reference the time cost of finding scattered assets: "you've searched this before"
- Connect to Audio Forge and Creator Panel: "assets you publish in Audio Forge appear in Asset Manager"

**Short description:**
> "All your assets. One place. Versioned and ready to deploy."

**Medium description:**
> "Asset Manager ends the tab-hopping that comes with a growing asset library. Every model, sound, script, and image — in one searchable repository with version history. Stop retracing steps to find something you built six months ago."

---

### Creator Panel — copy rules

**Creator Panel is not described like a product. It is described like a destination.**

The distinction is important. Every other product is described by what it does. Creator Panel is described by what it gives access to — which is everything. Its copy is about the command center experience, not about features.

**Copy rules for Creator Panel:**
- Never list Creator Panel's features alongside other products
- Always frame it as the hub: "where you access" not "what it does"
- The primary emotional note is control: a creator in the panel is a creator who has operational visibility
- Reference the dashboard view: "see your operation" before "manage your tools"
- Use "command center" language sparingly — but when used, it is the right word

**Short description (in ecosystem context):**
> "Your creator command center. Everything you operate in one place."

**Onboarding first sentence:**
> "This is where your creator operations live. Connect your identity to begin."

**What to avoid:**
- "Admin panel" — sounds like a back-end tool, not a creator workspace
- "Dashboard" alone — too generic; always qualify: "creator dashboard"
- Listing features: "Creator Panel includes X, Y, Z" — feature list reduces a hub to a product

---

### How complexity is hidden

The rule for all product copy: if the creator does not need to understand how something works to use it, do not explain how it works.

Creator Identity uses OAuth. License Manager queries an identity graph. Audio Forge communicates with Roblox's asset publication API. None of this should appear in product copy.

**The test:** Does knowing this mechanism help the creator use the product? If no, it belongs in the documentation. If yes, it can appear in product copy — but only as much as needed to make the product's behavior believable.

## Section 10 — Platform Copy System

### How Hibob Studio itself is described

Platform copy is different from product copy. Product copy describes what an individual product does. Platform copy describes what the sum of products does — which is something no individual product can do alone.

Platform copy answers the question: "What is Hibob Studio?"

The answer is always: "Creator infrastructure for Roblox developers." Everything else is elaboration.

---

### Three levels of copy

**Platform copy:** Describes Hibob Studio as a category and a company.

> "Hibob Studio is the creator infrastructure platform for serious Roblox developers. We build the tools, systems, and ecosystem that let you operate professionally."

Platform copy is used:
- Homepage hero
- About page
- Meta descriptions
- Social media profile bios
- Any context where someone asks "what is Hibob Studio?"

**Product copy:** Describes one specific product — what it does, what changes, what's possible. All product copy connects back to the platform ("accessible from Creator Panel," "powered by Creator Identity").

Product copy is used:
- Individual product pages
- Product cards in ecosystem overview
- Panel product onboarding
- Product-specific marketing

**Feature copy:** Describes one specific capability of one specific product — the most granular level.

> "Batch processing — queue up to 50 audio tracks in a single Forge session."

Feature copy is used:
- Plans comparison table (feature rows)
- Product page feature breakdowns
- Documentation
- Tooltip copy in the panel

---

### The rules for platform copy

**Never describe the platform by listing products.** "Hibob Studio includes Audio Forge, Asset Manager, License Manager, Donation Platform, Creator Identity, Creator Panel, Creator Plans, and API" is not platform copy — it is a product list. Platform copy describes what the sum achieves.

**Always describe the platform in terms of the creator's operational reality.** "Hibob Studio replaces the scattered manual workflows that consume serious Roblox creators' time" is platform copy. It describes the macro change, not the features.

**Platform copy can be humble.** The platform is early. Claims should match reality. "We're building creator infrastructure" is honest and strong. "We've built the definitive creator infrastructure" may not yet be true.

## Section 11 — Trust Copy System

### How trust is communicated

Trust in writing is built through four mechanisms: specificity, restraint, attribution, and accuracy. A fifth mechanism — consistency — is built over time.

**Specificity:** Specific claims are more credible than general ones. "847 audio tracks published in the platform's first week" is more credible than "hundreds of creators trust Hibob Studio." The general claim invites skepticism. The specific claim invites verification.

**Restraint:** Not claiming more than can be proven. When a platform is early-stage, the temptation is to use scale language prematurely. Resist it. A real small number is more trustworthy than a vague large claim.

**Attribution:** Claims about the platform are most credible when made by someone other than Hibob Studio. Statistics should cite their measurement period. Testimonials should attribute to a named, verifiable creator. Integration badges should be for integrations that exist.

**Accuracy:** Copy that was accurate when written must be updated when it is no longer accurate. A stat that says "30 active creators" when there are now 200 is not a conservative estimate — it is outdated copy that signals neglect.

---

### Trust copy for each trust signal type

**Statistics:**

Format: [Number] + [Unit] + [Timeframe if relevant]

Correct:
> "847 audio tracks published through Audio Forge"
> "32 developers with active license protection"
> "5,200 community donations processed"

Incorrect:
> "Thousands of satisfied creators" (unverifiable, smells fabricated)
> "100% satisfaction rate" (meaningless and unverifiable)
> "Growing community" (says nothing)

**Testimonials:**

Requirements for a testimonial to be used:
- Real creator, identified by name
- Verifiable Roblox or Discord presence
- Describes a specific product or workflow change, not general praise
- Does not describe a commission relationship (these are the old testimonials — retire them)

Template structure:
> "[Specific workflow description]. [Specific outcome.] — [Name], [Roblox role/context]"

Correct:
> "I used to spend Sunday evening uploading audio for the next week's club sessions. Audio Forge cut that to fifteen minutes. I haven't done it manually since." — Maxxx Salvatore, Club Map Owner

Incorrect:
> "Great tool, highly recommend." — Anonymous Creator
> "Hibob Studio is amazing!" — happy customer

**Integration trust:**

List only integrations that exist and work. Show Discord and Roblox logos because those integrations are real. Do not show integrations that are planned but not yet built — this will be discovered when a creator tries to use them.

**Ownership / IP trust:**

For License Manager specifically, the trust claim is that creator's intellectual property is protected. This must be stated carefully — License Manager reduces unauthorized redistribution, it does not eliminate it. Copy should say "protect" not "prevent entirely."

---

### Avoiding fake authority

**Fake authority looks like:**
- Award badges from publications the creator community does not know or trust
- Certifications that mean nothing to a Roblox creator
- Comparisons to larger platforms without specific differentiators
- "Industry-leading" without defining the industry or the metric

**Real authority looks like:**
- The product itself — a working platform is the strongest authority signal
- Real creator adoption numbers
- Named, verifiable testimonials
- Demonstrated Roblox-specific knowledge in the copy itself

## Section 12 — Pricing Copy System

### How plans are described

Plans are described as access levels, not price points. The headline of a pricing section is about what the creator gains access to — not what they pay. The price appears, clearly and prominently, but it is not the organizing principle.

**Tier description structure:**
1. Tier name (signals creator progression level)
2. Who it's for (one sentence — the specific creator profile this tier serves)
3. What it includes (4-6 specific feature inclusions — not an exhaustive list)
4. Price (monthly and annual if both exist)
5. CTA

---

### How upgrades are described

Upgrade prompts should not create anxiety. The most common mistake in upgrade copy is implying that the current plan is insufficient — this makes the user feel like they chose wrong.

Instead, upgrade prompts describe expanded capability, not current limitation.

**Incorrect:**
> "You've hit the limit on your current plan. Upgrade to continue."

**Correct:**
> "Ready for more capacity? Creator plan includes [X] that Studio tier expands."

The creator chose the correct plan for their situation at the time. When their situation changes, the upgrade reflects their growth — not their past error.

---

### How limitations are described

Every plan has limitations. These must be stated clearly — not hidden in footnotes, not euphemized, not buried in comparison tables.

A creator who discovers an undisclosed limitation after purchase will not trust the platform again. A creator who knew about a limitation when they subscribed will plan around it.

**Limitation copy rules:**
- State limitations in the same section where the feature is claimed
- Use plain language: "Up to 50 tracks per Forge session in the Creator plan"
- Do not use "only" as a modifier: "only 50 tracks" implies scarcity-as-inadequacy. "Up to 50 tracks" is a statement of scope.
- If a limitation is likely to affect the creator's use case, it must be visible before the CTA

---

### How value is framed

The reference point for pricing copy is not the price — it is what the price replaces.

A Creator Plan that costs Rp X per month is not evaluated against "is Rp X per month expensive?" It is evaluated against: "How much time does this save me? What does that time cost?"

**Framing that helps:**

> "Less than an hour of developer work per month — and it replaces hours of manual overhead every week."

This framing is not about the price. It is about the value the creator already understands — their time has a cost, and the plan costs less than that.

**What to avoid in pricing copy:**
- "Affordable" — this word signals that the writer knows the price is sensitive
- "Just Rp X" — "just" minimizes the price, which implies the price needs minimizing
- Comparing to competitors by price unless the comparison is specific and fair
- "No hidden fees" — this implies there are fees to hide until stated otherwise

## Section 13 — Errors to Avoid

These are the most common copy failures in the context of Hibob Studio's positioning transition. Each one would undermine the platform positioning if it appears in published copy.

---

### Freelancer language

**What it looks like and why it fails:**

> "Let me help you build your Roblox game." — "let me" implies a personal service relationship  
> "Reach out and we can discuss your project." — "project" implies commission  
> "I've built over 9 games on Roblox." — first person + portfolio claim  
> "We take pride in delivering quality work." — delivery implies services  

Every instance of this language type signals the old positioning to the reader. A creator who sees this language will not form the mental model "this is a platform" — they will form the mental model "this is a freelancer." Undoing that mental model requires the creator to read more copy that contradicts it. Better to never create it.

---

### Agency language

> "End-to-end solutions for your Roblox development needs" — agency packaging  
> "Full-service creator support" — "full-service" is agency positioning  
> "We manage your workflow" — Hibob Studio manages nothing; creators manage their own workflow  
> "Your dedicated creator success team" — implies human service layer  

Agency language positions Hibob Studio as a service provider with humans doing work for creators. This is not the model. The platform does things automatically. The creator does things themselves, using the platform.

---

### Corporate buzzwords

These words signal that the writer was writing to fill space, not to communicate:

> "Our comprehensive, integrated platform ecosystem solution..."  
> "Leveraging synergistic creator workflows..."  
> "Empowering the next generation of Roblox innovators..."  

If a sentence sounds like it was written by someone who has never used Roblox and was told to "write something that sounds professional," it is corporate buzzword copy. Delete it. Start over with the specific thing you are trying to say.

---

### AI-generated sounding copy

The tells are consistent: over-qualified statements, hedged claims, transitions that go nowhere, sentences that restate the previous sentence in different words.

> "In the dynamic and ever-evolving landscape of Roblox game development, creators face numerous challenges that can impact their productivity and creative output. Hibob Studio is here to address these challenges with our innovative suite of tools designed specifically for the Roblox creator community."

This is four sentences that say one thing: "Roblox creators have problems. Hibob Studio helps." Write the two sentences. Not the four.

**The test:** If a sentence removes all its own specificity, what is left? "Creators face challenges" — yes, that is always true. The sentence has said nothing. Rewrite.

---

### Feature dumping

Feature dumping is describing every capability of a product in the first paragraph. The reader's brain cannot process a list of ten features and form a clear understanding of what the product does.

**Feature dump:**
> "Audio Forge includes batch processing, moderation queue management, asset ID tracking, folder input, preview mode, export formats, history log, platform sync, Discord notifications, and automated error handling."

**Correct approach:**
Start with the workflow change. Mention the two or three most important features. Link to documentation or the product page for full feature detail.

> "Audio Forge processes an entire audio folder in one session — tracks go in, asset IDs come out. Batch processing handles the Roblox moderation queue automatically. Full feature list in the docs."

---

### Overpromising

Claiming outcomes the platform cannot reliably deliver:

> "Never deal with audio publishing problems again." — there will be Roblox moderation issues  
> "Your products will be completely protected from piracy." — piracy will still exist  
> "License Manager eliminates unauthorized use." — it reduces it significantly but cannot eliminate it  

Overpromising creates a specific kind of trust failure: the creator encounters a case where the promise wasn't kept, and the entire platform's credibility is damaged. Precise, accurate claims build durable trust. Ambitious claims build short-term impressions and long-term resentment.

---

## Section 14 — Microcopy System

Microcopy is the small-scale writing that surrounds interactive elements: buttons, forms, tooltips, empty states, success states, error states, loading states. These are not afterthoughts. In a platform experience, microcopy is often the most-read copy on the entire interface.

---

### Buttons

**Rules:**
- Verb-first: "Open," "Start," "Connect," "Save," "Try," "Explore"
- Never passive: "Click here," "Proceed," "Submit" (submit implies forms, not platform actions)
- Match the action to the verb precisely: "Publish tracks" not "Confirm" for the Audio Forge publish action
- Maximum 4 words on a button
- No exclamation points

| Action | Good button copy | Bad button copy |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| Begin panel access | "Open Creator Panel" | "Get Started!" |
| Start Audio Forge session | "Start Forge Session" | "Upload Audio" |
| Connect creator identity | "Connect Identity" | "Link Accounts" |
| Subscribe to a plan | "Start Creator Plan" | "Purchase" |
| Confirm license purchase | "Confirm License" | "Submit Order" |

---

### Forms

**Rules:**
- Label every field. Never rely on placeholder text as a label — it disappears when the creator starts typing.
- Use field labels that describe what to enter, not what the field is called: "Your Discord username" not "Discord field"
- Validation messages appear in real-time where possible — not only on submission
- Required fields are marked; optional fields are indicated as "(optional)" not required fields marked with asterisks that aren't explained
- Long forms must explain why each piece of information is needed if the reason isn't obvious

**Field label examples:**

| Field | Good label | Bad label |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Creator's Discord | "Discord username (e.g., username#0001)" | "Discord*" |
| Roblox username | "Roblox display name" | "Username" |
| License key | "Your license key (found in your order confirmation)" | "Key" |

---

### Tooltips

**Rules:**
- Tooltips explain the non-obvious. They do not explain the obvious.
- Maximum 1-2 sentences. If a tooltip requires more, the UI needs redesign.
- Write tooltips in the same voice as all copy — not more formal, not more casual
- Tooltips should answer "what does this do" or "why do I need this" — not both in one tooltip

**Examples:**

Creator Identity connection tooltip:
> "Linking your Discord and Roblox accounts lets the platform verify your buyers, donors, and community members automatically."

Audio Forge batch size tooltip:
> "Maximum tracks per session depends on your Creator Plan tier. View plan details for your current limit."

---

### Success states

Success states should feel warm but brief. The creator accomplished something — acknowledge it without over-celebrating.

**Rules:**
- Confirm what happened specifically: "Identity connected" not "Success!"
- Include the next step when relevant
- Keep it short — success states that require reading feel like they're explaining why the user succeeded, which is condescending

**Examples:**

| Action | Good success message | Bad success message |
|--------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Identity connected | "Identity connected. You now have access to the full platform." | "Congratulations! You've successfully linked your accounts!" |
| Audio forge complete | "24 tracks published. Asset IDs saved to Asset Manager." | "Upload complete! Your files are ready!" |
| License issued | "License issued. [Buyer name] has been notified." | "Great job! Payment processed successfully!" |

---

### Error states

Error states should tell the creator what happened and what to do next — not apologize excessively or create anxiety.

**Rules:**
- Name what failed specifically: "Audio Forge could not connect to Roblox" not "An error occurred"
- Give a next step: "Check your Roblox API key in settings, then try again" not "Please try again later"
- Never blame the creator: "Could not connect" not "You entered an invalid key"
- Avoid "Oops" — it's casual in a way that doesn't match the seriousness of the creator's workflow disruption

**Examples:**

| Error | Good error message | Bad error message |
|-------|-------------------|------------------|
| Identity connection failed | "Could not connect to Discord. Check that you've authorized the Hibob Studio app in Discord settings, then try again." | "Oops! Something went wrong. Please try again." |
| Forge session failed | "Audio Forge could not process 3 tracks. Check that the file formats are .mp3 or .ogg and try again." | "Upload failed. Please try again." |
| License issue failed | "License could not be issued. Verify that the buyer's identity is connected and try again." | "An error has occurred. Contact support." |

---

### Loading states

Loading states exist to manage expectation during wait time. They should be informative when the wait is significant.

**Rules:**
- For fast operations (<2 seconds): simple progress indicator, no copy needed
- For medium operations (2-10 seconds): "Processing..." or specific action: "Forging audio..."
- For long operations (>10 seconds): explain what's happening: "Publishing to Roblox — this typically takes 30-60 seconds per track."

**Examples:**

| Operation | Loading copy |
|-----------|-------------|
| Audio Forge processing | "Forging audio — publishing to Roblox..." |
| Identity verification | "Verifying identity across Discord and Roblox..." |
| License issuance | "Issuing license to [buyer]..." |

---

### Empty states

Empty states are the most neglected surfaces in platform copy. When a creator first arrives and has no data, the empty state is their introduction to what the product does.

**Rules:**
- Describe what will appear here once data exists: "Your published tracks will appear here once you run your first Forge session."
- Include a direct path to create the first entry: "Start your first Forge session →"
- Never show a bare empty state with no guidance — it makes the platform feel broken

**Examples:**

| Empty state | Good copy | Bad copy |
|------------|-----------|---------|
| Audio Forge (no tracks) | "No tracks published yet. Start a Forge session to publish your first batch." [Start Forge Session →] | "No results found." |
| License Manager (no licenses) | "No active licenses. List a product to start issuing licenses automatically." [List a Product →] | "You have no licenses." |
| Creator Panel (new user) | "Your creator infrastructure is ready. Connect your identity to unlock the full platform." [Connect Identity →] | "Welcome to Creator Panel." |

## Section 15 — Panel Copy System

### The principle

`panel.hibobstudio.com` is the Creator Command Center. Every word in the panel should reinforce this identity — not because the phrase "command center" should be used repeatedly, but because every piece of copy should feel like it belongs in a professional operational workspace.

The panel does not feel like a website. It does not feel like an admin backend. It feels like a place where serious creative work gets managed.

---

### Language principles for the panel

**Operations language, not service language.**

The panel is where the creator operates. Not where they request services. Not where they configure settings (though settings exist). Not where they manage a portfolio.

| Service/admin language | Operations language |
|-----------------------|--------------------|
| "Manage your account" | "Your creator workspace" |
| "Settings" | "Settings" (settings is fine — but the section around it should not feel like an admin panel) |
| "User profile" | "Creator profile" |
| "Dashboard" alone | "Creator overview" or "Operations overview" |
| "Admin tools" | Remove entirely — the creator is operating, not administering |

**Status language, not feature language.**

The panel's primary view should show operational status — what is happening, what is pending, what needs attention. The copy should reflect this: "Active licenses: 47" is status language. "License Manager — available" is feature language.

**Present tense, active state.**

The panel describes the current state of the creator's operations. Present tense. Active voice.

> "3 Forge sessions running."  
> "12 licenses issued this week."  
> "Identity connected. All systems active."  

Not:
> "Your account is set up."  
> "You can use the following features."  
> "Audio Forge is available in your plan."  

---

### Onboarding copy rules

The panel's first-run experience is the most important panel copy. It sets the entire mental model of what the platform is for.

**First screen (pre-identity connection):**
> "Connect your identity to begin."  

Not: "Welcome to Hibob Studio Creator Panel! Let's get you set up."

The creator doesn't need to be welcomed. They need to begin. The copy assumes competence and signals that the first action is meaningful.

**Identity connection prompt:**
> "Creator Identity links your Discord and Roblox accounts. Every product in the platform uses this to verify who you are — and who your buyers and community members are."

This copy is allowed to be slightly longer because it explains the why — which the creator needs to understand before they will complete the action.

**Post-identity connection:**
> "Identity connected. You're now recognized across the platform. Explore your products below."

Brief. Specific. Points forward.

---

### Navigation labels in the panel

Panel navigation should use operation-oriented labels — nouns that name the creator's operational domain, not the product's features.

| Standard (avoid) | Operations-oriented (use) |
|-----------------|--------------------------|
| Audio Manager | Audio Forge |
| Upload Center | Forge Studio (or simply: Audio Forge) |
| License Dashboard | Active Licenses |
| Community Management | Creator Community |
| Settings | Settings (acceptable as-is) |
| Account | Creator Profile |

## Section 16 — Product Naming Rules

### Naming hierarchy

Hibob Studio uses a **Branded House** model (established in positioning.md §17). Every product carries the Hibob Studio parent brand.

**Formal naming convention:**
- Hibob Studio Creator Panel
- Hibob Studio Audio Forge
- Hibob Studio Asset Manager
- Hibob Studio License Manager
- Hibob Studio Donation Platform
- Hibob Studio Creator Identity
- Hibob Studio Creator Plans
- Hibob Studio API

**Informal reference (acceptable in community, Discord, casual contexts):**
- Creator Panel
- Audio Forge
- Asset Manager
- License Manager
- Donation Platform
- Creator Identity
- Creator Plans
- API

**Minimum acceptable in formal context:** Product name alone ("Audio Forge") is acceptable when the surrounding context makes clear the Hibob Studio affiliation. "Hibob Studio Audio Forge" is required in contexts where the reader might not know the parent brand.

---

### How current products are named — the pattern

Current product names follow a pattern worth understanding:

- **Function + Tool:** Audio Forge (audio + transformation), Asset Manager (asset + management), License Manager (license + management)
- **Function + Platform:** Donation Platform (donation + infrastructure)
- **Creator + Function:** Creator Identity, Creator Panel, Creator Plans (all use "Creator" prefix to signal audience alignment and brand consistency)
- **Simple descriptor:** API

The pattern reveals: names are either function-first (what the product does) or audience-first (who the product is for). Both approaches work — but mixing them randomly creates a name catalog that feels arbitrary.

---

### Rules for naming future products

**Rule 1 — Name from the creator's perspective, not the engineering perspective.**

Engineering names: "Whitelist Manager," "Identity Resolver," "Content Pipeline"
Creator perspective names: "License Manager," "Creator Identity," "Audio Forge"

The engineering name describes the system. The creator name describes what changes for the creator.

**Rule 2 — Maintain the Branded House.**

New products are Hibob Studio [Name], not standalone brands. No future product should have an identity strong enough to be confused for a company — it is always a product within the Hibob Studio ecosystem.

**Rule 3 — One or two words per product name.**

Current names average two words. This is the right range. Three-word product names are difficult to recall and difficult to design around. "Hibob Studio Advanced Creator Analytics Platform" is not a product name.

**Rule 4 — Prefer concrete nouns over abstractions.**

"Audio Forge" — concrete (audio) + action (forge)  
"Creator Identity" — concrete (creator) + concept (identity)  
"Asset Manager" — concrete (asset) + concrete (manager)  

Avoid abstract concept-only names: "Nexus," "Orbit," "Core" — these are meaningless without context and do not communicate function.

**Rule 5 — Test the name in a sentence.**

"I use Audio Forge to publish my tracks." — works  
"I use Asset Manager to organize my models." — works  
"I use Creator Identity to verify my buyers." — works  

If the product name reads naturally in an explanation sentence, it passes. If it requires interpretation, reconsider.

## Section 17 — Examples

Real examples of copy that is on-voice vs. off-voice for Hibob Studio. These are reference calibrations — not final copy.

## Section 19 — Final Writing Principles

The ten non-negotiable rules for all Hibob Studio writing. These apply to every surface, every context, every writer.

---

**1. The platform does not sell time.**
Hibob Studio does not do things for creators. The platform enables creators to do things for themselves — faster, automatically, with less friction. Every sentence that implies Hibob Studio is performing a service must be rewritten to show the platform enabling the creator's own action.

**2. Specificity is always more credible than superlatives.**
"30 audio tracks in 10 minutes" is more credible than "incredibly fast audio publishing." The specific claim can be verified. The superlative cannot. When in doubt, get specific.

**3. Name the problem before the solution — every time.**
The creator already knows the problem. The platform is the solution. Lead with what the creator knows (problem), then deliver what they need to learn (solution). This sequence builds resonance before it builds understanding.

**4. The creator is the subject. The platform is the verb.**
In good Hibob Studio copy, the creator does things — enabled by the platform. "You publish audio in batches" not "Audio Forge publishes audio for you." The creator is in control. The platform gives them that control.

**5. Write at the level of the reader, not the level of the product.**
The reader is a Roblox developer who understands Luau, Roblox moderation, asset IDs, and group systems. Write accordingly. Do not explain what a Roblox asset ID is. Do not explain what Discord roles are. Do not use analogies that talk down to the reader.

**6. One idea per sentence.**
Complex ideas are expressed through a sequence of simple sentences, not through a single complex one. "Audio Forge processes your tracks in batch. Each track is published to Roblox automatically. You receive a complete list of asset IDs." Three sentences, three ideas, no confusion.

**7. Active voice is not optional.**
"The license is issued automatically" → "License Manager issues the license automatically." The platform is active. Passive voice makes the platform sound inert. Hibob Studio's products do things — the writing must show that.

**8. Coming Soon is not "available."**
Products in development are labeled Coming Soon. They are not described as though they are available. A creator who reads "Coming Soon" and a creator who reads (implied) "available" but discovers it isn't will have very different trust reactions. Label accurately.

**9. No copy is permanent.**
Copy must be updated when it becomes inaccurate. Statistics age. Features ship. Plans change. A document or page with outdated claims signals neglect — which is a trust signal in the wrong direction. Build a review schedule. Update when things change.

**10. If it could be written by someone who has never used Roblox, rewrite it.**
Hibob Studio's differentiation is Roblox-native depth. That depth must be audible in the writing. If the copy could describe a tool for YouTube creators, Minecraft players, or indie game developers without changing a word, it is not specific enough for Hibob Studio.

**"Hibob Studio is the infrastructure that serious Roblox creators use to stop wasting time on operations and start spending it on building."**
