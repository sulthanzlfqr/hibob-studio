# Hibob Studio — Homepage Blueprint V2

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 2026-05-30  
**Source of Truth:** positioning.md v1.0 + information-architecture-v2.md v1.0  
**Status:** Blueprint — Source of truth for copywriting, design, and implementation

> This document defines the homepage of hibobstudio.com as a platform homepage — not a portfolio, not a commission page, not a personal site. Every decision in this document derives from the positioning and information architecture already established. When a design or copy decision conflicts with this document, this document takes precedence.

---

## Section 1 — Homepage Objective

### Primary Goal

Convert cold visitors — creators who have never heard of Hibob Studio — into Creator Panel sessions.

The homepage does not exist to inform. Information is what the individual product pages are for. The homepage exists to earn the right to send a creator to `panel.hibobstudio.com` — and to make them arrive there with the correct mental model already formed.

Every section, every line of copy, every visual element on the homepage is either moving a visitor closer to that panel session or it is wasting their attention.

---

### Secondary Goal

Establish Hibob Studio as the name for a category that the creator already knows they need but did not have a name for.

Most serious Roblox creators know their workflow is broken. They do not call it "lacking creator infrastructure" — they call it "I hate uploading audio one by one" or "I spent three hours whitelisting buyers again." The homepage's secondary job is to give this unnamed frustration a named solution — and make that solution's name "Hibob Studio."

This is category creation, not product marketing. The creator who leaves the homepage saying "that's what I've been missing" and remembering the name "Hibob Studio" has been converted at the positioning level, regardless of whether they clicked to the panel that session.

---

### Success Criteria

**Quantitative (once tracking is in place):**
- Bounce rate below the industry benchmark for B2B SaaS landing pages
- "Open Creator Panel" click-through rate as the primary conversion metric
- Scroll depth past the Problem section — indicating the visitor recognized their situation
- Time on homepage: 2+ minutes indicates engagement, not confusion

**Qualitative:**
- A creator who reads the page should be able to explain what Hibob Studio is without re-reading it
- A creator should be able to identify which product is most relevant to them without navigating away from the homepage
- No creator should finish reading the homepage believing this is a freelance commission service

---

### Visitor Understanding at Key Moments

**After 10 seconds** (first fold only):

The visitor knows one thing: *Hibob Studio is a platform for serious Roblox creators.*

They do not need to know the product names. They do not need to understand the ecosystem. They do not need to know the pricing. They need to know the category. The entire first fold — headline, subheadline, badge, CTA — is built to deliver this one recognition.

If a visitor at 10 seconds cannot tell a friend "it's some kind of platform for Roblox creators," the hero has failed.

**After 30 seconds** (hero + problem section):

The visitor knows: *This platform exists because my workflow is broken in ways I've felt but couldn't name.*

At 30 seconds, the creator has moved from "what is this?" to "this is relevant to me." The Problem section creates this shift. The creator recognizes at least one specific frustration in Hibob Studio's description of the current state: uploading audio manually, whitelisting buyers by hand, managing identity across Discord and Roblox without a system.

Recognition is the mechanism that turns a cold visitor into an engaged reader.

**After 2 minutes** (through ecosystem + product spotlight):

The visitor knows: *This platform contains multiple tools that work together, one or two of which would immediately improve my workflow, and I need to get into the panel to access them.*

At 2 minutes, the creator has enough information to make a directional decision: "I should try this" or "this is not for me right now." The homepage should make both of these decisions easy — the converter gets a clear path to the panel, the non-converter leaves with a correct impression of what Hibob Studio is for when they return later.

## Section 2 — Page Narrative

The homepage is a story, not a brochure. It has a beginning, a middle, and an end. The story follows a specific arc: a creator's problem is named → a platform claims to solve it → the platform proves it can → the creator is invited to experience it.

Every section exists because it answers a question the creator is asking at that specific point in their reading. Remove any section, and the creator's journey has a gap. Add any section that doesn't answer a question the creator is asking at that moment, and the narrative loses momentum.

---

### The story, section by section:

**First pixel — the creator arrives.** They come from Discord, from a friend's recommendation, from search. They have no context. They need a category. The hero delivers it in 3 seconds or less. No preamble. No loading screens that delay the message. The platform claims its category from the first moment.

**Why do they need a category first?** Because everything else — products, features, pricing — is evaluated through the frame of the category. A creator who misidentifies Hibob Studio as a freelancer service will evaluate the product cards as a commission menu. A creator who correctly identifies it as a platform will evaluate the same cards as tools to adopt. The frame comes first.

**After the hero — the creator has the category but needs resonance.** Saying "Creator Infrastructure Platform" sets the category. But it doesn't make the creator feel anything. The Problem section is where they feel it. The problem section names specific, daily frustrations that the creator has experienced. When the creator reads something that describes their exact experience — uploading audio one file at a time, verifying buyers manually, opening five different tabs to manage one community — they stop evaluating and start reading. Resonance is the bridge between category and commitment.

**After resonance comes scope.** The creator now believes this platform might be for them. The question they ask next is "how much does this solve?" The Ecosystem section answers this by showing all eight products in a connected map. The visual must communicate that this is a system — not a menu. The creator should feel that adopting Hibob Studio means all of these operational problems get addressed, not just one.

**After scope comes specificity.** The creator has seen the map. Now they want to find themselves on it. The Product Spotlight section zooms in on the one or two products most relevant to the broadest swath of the primary audience. This is not a feature list — it is a before/after. Before Audio Forge: 2 hours of manual uploading. After Audio Forge: 10 minutes for the same output. Specificity converts interested visitors into intentional ones.

**After specificity comes proof.** The creator has understood what the platform does. Now they want to know if it actually works. Social Proof provides this evidence: platform statistics, identifiable creator testimonials, integration logos that confirm the platform operates in a real ecosystem. This section is not the centerpiece — it is the validator.

**After proof comes commitment decision.** The creator is ready to evaluate cost against perceived value. The Plans section appears here — after the product has been understood and validated, but before the final CTA. Pricing at this moment feels like access, not expense. The creator is not asking "can I afford this?" they are asking "which level of access do I want?"

**The final section — invitation.** The creator has seen the category, felt the resonance, understood the scope, identified their specific product, seen the proof, and evaluated the cost. Now they need one clear action. The final section is an invitation with no competing options. One destination: Creator Panel. One message: start now.

## Section 3 — Hero Blueprint

### Purpose

The hero performs one job: declare the category and make the creator feel that this platform is specifically for them — in under 3 seconds.

The hero is not a welcome message. It is not a company introduction. It is not a feature list. It is a category declaration aimed at a specific audience, delivered with confidence.

Everything in the hero is subordinated to this purpose. If an element in the hero does not contribute to "what is this" and "is this for me," it should not be in the hero.

---

### Emotional Goal

The emotional experience at the end of the hero section should be: **recognition.**

Not excitement. Not urgency. Not awe. Recognition — the specific feeling a creator gets when they realize someone has named and understood a problem they have been living with.

"This was built for me" is the emotional target. Not "this looks impressive" and not "this seems interesting." The creator should feel understood before they feel sold to.

---

### Business Goal

Move the creator from cold visitor to engaged reader. The hero's business job is not conversion — it is retention of attention long enough for the problem section to do its work.

The secondary business goal: establish "Hibob Studio" as the name of the category in the creator's memory. A creator who bounces from the homepage but remembers the name and what it does is a delayed conversion, not a lost one.

---

### Key Messages (in priority order)

**Message 1 (must be in headline):** Hibob Studio is a platform — specifically an infrastructure platform — for serious Roblox creators.

**Message 2 (must be in subheadline or supporting copy):** The platform solves the operational overhead that consumes creator time: audio management, asset control, identity, licensing, community monetization.

**Message 3 (implied by visual and layout):** This is a real, functional platform — not a personal project. The visual weight, the layout structure, and the UI aesthetics must communicate "this is built to be used professionally."

---

### Trust Elements

Trust elements in the hero are micro-signals — they take 1-2 seconds to process and their job is to reduce the instinct to bounce, not to build deep trust.

**Recommended trust elements (choose 2-3, not all):**

1. **Quantified platform statistic** — A real number that shows adoption. "X active creators" or "Y products deployed." Must be accurate. Even a small real number is more credible than a vague large claim. Positioned near the CTA or below the headline.

2. **Integration icons** — Discord logo + Roblox logo positioned visibly in the hero. These are trust anchors for this specific audience — they immediately communicate that the platform operates in their ecosystem.

3. **Platform badge / eyebrow label** — A category label above the headline: "Creator Infrastructure Platform" or "Built for Roblox Creators." This badge performs the category declaration before the headline even starts.

4. **Creator Panel preview** — A visual representation of the Creator Panel dashboard. Not a full screenshot — a fragment, a glimpse. The goal is to show that the platform exists and has a real interface.

**What should not be in hero trust signals:**
- Star ratings without context
- "Trusted by X companies" (this is a platform for individual creators, not companies)
- Long testimonial quote (too much reading at first glance)
- Award badges that mean nothing to the Roblox creator audience

---

### Primary CTA

**Destination:** `panel.hibobstudio.com`  
**Intent:** Begin the creator's first session with the platform  
**Design:** High visual priority — color-filled button, prominent placement below headline, clear label

The primary CTA label must communicate access, not action-on-the-website. Examples of correct framing: "Open Creator Panel," "Access the Platform," "Start Building." Examples of incorrect framing: "Hire Me," "Get Started" (too generic), "Sign Up" (sounds like a newsletter).

The most specific label that accurately describes what happens when the button is clicked is the best label.

---

### Secondary CTA

**Destination:** Smooth scroll to Ecosystem section (anchor: `#ecosystem`)  
**Intent:** For creators who want to understand before committing  
**Design:** Lower visual priority than primary — outline or ghost button style, clearly subordinate

The secondary CTA exists for the creator who thinks "I want to understand what I'm getting into before I open anything." It answers their hesitation without losing them. It does not compete with the primary CTA — it serves a different need.

The secondary CTA label must describe movement, not passivity. "See the ecosystem" or "Explore the platform" rather than "Learn more" (destination-less) or "Read more" (passive).

**Maximum of two CTAs in the hero.** Any additional CTA in the hero creates decision paralysis and reduces conversion rate on both CTAs.

---

### Visual Direction

The hero visual tells a story with its composition, not just with its content. The current hero (developer character illustration with floating animation) tells the story of an individual. The new hero must tell the story of a system.

**Direction:** The dominant visual in the hero should be a representation of the Creator Panel — either a mockup, a stylized screenshot, or a diagram that shows the command center concept. This is not decoration. This visual makes an argument: "the platform exists and has a real interface."

**Composition principle:** The left side carries the text (headline, subheadline, CTAs, trust signals). The right side carries the visual (Creator Panel representation). This is a standard platform-company homepage composition — it works because it separates the claim (left) from the evidence (right).

**Atmosphere:** The hero should feel like a tool you want to open, not a person you want to hire. Dark matte background, purple gradient accents, subtle grid or geometric pattern that suggests infrastructure — the aesthetic of a professional tool, not a personal portfolio.

**What the hero should NOT show:**
- A character or avatar illustration (this is a person-forward visual, not a platform-forward visual)
- Generic "abstract digital" art with no connection to the product
- A cluttered list of features or bullet points — these belong below the hero

## Section 4 — Platform Introduction

### The problem this section solves

After the hero, the creator understands the category but has not yet understood why a platform specifically exists for this problem. They may still be carrying the unconscious assumption that this is a freelancer with a professional-looking website.

The Platform Introduction section destroys that assumption by explicitly describing the operational problem that created the platform — in the creator's own language.

---

### Message

The message is not about Hibob Studio. The message is about the creator's current situation.

"You already know your workflow is broken. You've accepted it as normal. It isn't."

The section articulates — concretely and specifically — what a serious Roblox creator currently does manually every week: uploading audio one file at a time, verifying buyers by hand, switching between Discord, Roblox Studio, spreadsheets, and DMs to manage a single community. Not in abstract terms. In specific, recognizable, daily terms.

Then — and only then — it introduces the platform as the answer. One sentence: "Hibob Studio is the infrastructure layer that replaces all of this."

**The structure is deliberately empathetic-first:**
- First: describe the problem the creator lives with
- Second: validate that it costs them time and energy
- Third: introduce the solution

The sequence matters. A creator who sees the solution before they see their problem reflected back to them is being sold to. A creator who sees their problem named first is being understood. Understood creators convert; sold-to creators bounce.

---

### Visual Direction

The visual for this section should contrast with the hero's aspirational energy. Where the hero shows what's possible (the platform, the panel), the Problem section shows what's current (fragmentation, manual work, scattered tools).

**Visual approach — contrast diagram:**
Left column labeled "Without Hibob Studio": a scattered cluster of icons representing current tools — Google Drive, Discord, spreadsheet, Roblox tab, DM, manual upload. Arrows pointing in different directions. The visual should feel chaotic but recognizable.

Right column labeled "With Hibob Studio": one icon — the Creator Panel — with all other functions flowing into it. The visual should feel resolved and organized.

This contrast communicates the core value proposition without requiring the visitor to read anything. It works for creators who are skimming and for creators who are reading.

**Alternative visual approach:** A scrolling or animated representation of the creator's daily manual process — each task appearing sequentially as a line item with time cost. "Upload audio track 1... 4 minutes. Upload audio track 2... 4 minutes. Upload audio track 3... 4 minutes." This approach creates the fatigue of the problem viscerally, not just intellectually.

---

### User Takeaway

After reading (or even skimming) this section, the creator should:
1. Have their current situation described back to them accurately
2. Feel that Hibob Studio was built by someone who has lived this problem (not designed by someone who researched it)
3. Want to know what the solution actually looks like

This section is a setup. Its success is measured by whether the creator continues to the Ecosystem section — not by whether it converts on its own.

## Section 5 — Ecosystem Blueprint

### Narrative Flow

The Ecosystem section makes the most important structural argument of the homepage: these products are components of one system, not a collection of independent tools.

The section must be designed to be understood in two passes:
- **Visual pass (2 seconds):** Creator sees a connected structure and understands "these things are related"
- **Reading pass (30 seconds):** Creator understands which products belong to which layer and how they connect

**Beat 1 — The connector sentence:**
One sentence introduces the ecosystem before any product is named: "One platform. Eight tools. Built to work together." Or a variation. The point is to prime the visual that follows with the concept of connection.

**Beat 2 — The ecosystem visual:**
The centerpiece of this section is a visual hierarchy showing all eight products in their relationships. The layout communicates the architecture:

```
Foundation Layer
    [Creator Identity] — "Every product runs on your verified creator identity"

Hub Layer  
    [Creator Panel] — "Access and manage everything from one place"

Content Layer          Monetization Layer         Developer Layer
[Audio Forge]          [License Manager]          [API Infrastructure]
[Asset Manager]        [Donation Platform]
                       [Creator Plans]
```

The visual does not need to be a literal tree diagram — it can be a more designed representation. But the hierarchy and the connection must be visible and legible at a glance.

**Beat 3 — Product summaries:**
Below the visual map, or integrated into it, each product appears with:
- Product name
- One-line functional description (what it does, not what it is)
- Status badge: Available / Coming Soon

No feature lists. No pricing. No CTAs per product. Those belong on product pages.

**Beat 4 — The invitation:**
The section closes with a directional statement: "All of this lives in your Creator Panel." One CTA: "Explore Products" linking to `/products`.

---

### Hierarchy

**Creator Identity is not equal to other products — it is the foundation.**

The ecosystem visualization must make this clear. Creator Identity should appear at the base of the hierarchy, visually supporting everything above it. This is not just accurate architecture — it also sets up the onboarding expectation: "I need to connect my identity first to unlock the full platform."

**Creator Panel is not a product among products — it is the hub.**

Creator Panel should appear at the center or at the top of the hierarchy in a visually distinct position. It is where the other products are accessed. It is the entry point. Its visual position should communicate this.

**The four functional layers are peer layers:**

Content (Audio Forge, Asset Manager) and Monetization (License Manager, Donation Platform, Creator Plans) and Developer (API) are peer layers — none is more important than the others for all creators. The visual should not imply a hierarchy among these layers.

---

### Visual Storytelling

The ecosystem section's visual tells a specific story: **before Hibob Studio, these needs are scattered across different tools and platforms; after Hibob Studio, they are all accessible in one place.**

This is not stated — it is shown. The visual structure of the ecosystem is itself the argument.

**Connection lines or visual connectors** between products are not decorative — they are the argument. If the visual shows eight separate boxes with no visual connection, the visitor's brain concludes "these are eight separate products." If the visual shows eight products connected through Creator Panel, the brain concludes "this is a system."

**Color coding by layer** reinforces the architecture. Each layer (Identity, Hub, Content, Monetization, Developer) can have a distinct color treatment — allowing creators to pattern-match their specific problem to the relevant layer quickly.

**Status differentiation** for Coming Soon products should be present but not apologetic. A Coming Soon badge communicates roadmap transparency — a platform that shows you what it's building is more trustworthy than one that only shows what exists. Greyscale or reduced opacity for coming soon products is sufficient.

## Section 6 — Product Showcase Blueprint

The Product Showcase is where the homepage gets specific. The Ecosystem section showed the map. The Product Showcase zooms into the most important destinations on that map.

**Architecture decision:** Not all eight products get spotlight treatment on the homepage. The homepage features 2-3 products at spotlight depth. All products are represented in the Ecosystem section. Individual product pages carry the full depth for each product.

**Selection criteria for homepage spotlight:**
Products featured on the homepage should be selected by:
1. **Broadest relevance** — which products address pain points felt by the widest segment of the primary audience
2. **Clearest before/after** — which products have the most visceral workflow contrast (before vs. after)
3. **Lowest adoption barrier** — which products can be adopted with the least prerequisite setup

By this criteria: **Audio Forge** and **License Manager** are the recommended homepage spotlight products for V2.

---

### Audio Forge

**Purpose on homepage:**
Demonstrate the most universal workflow improvement Hibob Studio provides. Every game creator who uses music — which is the majority of the primary audience — has experienced the pain of audio publishing. Audio Forge converts this universal pain into a universal value proposition.

**Value to communicate:**
Before: Upload each audio track individually. Wait for moderation. Copy the asset ID. Repeat for every track. For a game with 20 tracks, this is hours.
After: Batch process. Automated pipeline. Asset IDs managed. Done in minutes.

The "hours vs. minutes" framing is the most immediately legible value proposition for this product. It does not require the creator to understand anything about the platform's architecture — it directly translates to their daily experience.

**Visual strategy:**
Show the contrast as a split view — the current experience (multiple browser tabs, manual upload UI, copy-paste asset IDs) vs. the Audio Forge experience (one interface, batch queue, automated output). The visual does the work that copy cannot: it shows what the workflow actually looks like.

**Conversion strategy:**
The showcase ends with one CTA: "Try Audio Forge" → leads to Creator Panel's Audio Forge section. Not to a product page (that's for visitors who need more depth). Not to a pricing page. To the panel — where they can experience it.

---

### License Manager

**Purpose on homepage:**
Demonstrate the most financially impactful workflow improvement. License Manager speaks to the creator who sells products — and the pain of manual buyer management is a story of lost money (time spent whitelisting = time not spent building) and risk (piracy, unauthorized redistribution).

**Value to communicate:**
Before: Receive payment → verify Discord identity → add Roblox username to whitelist manually → confirm with buyer → hope they don't share → repeat for every buyer.
After: Payment received → license issued automatically → access granted → done.

The value is not "efficiency" — the value is "you don't have to do this anymore." The emotional note is relief.

**Visual strategy:**
A flow diagram showing the before and after of a license transaction. The "before" flow has 7+ steps with manual checkpoints. The "after" flow has 3 automated steps. The visual is the argument: the length of the flow tells the story.

**Conversion strategy:**
CTA: "Protect your products" → leads to Creator Panel's License Manager section. The CTA label frames the action as protection (the creator's emotional need) rather than efficiency (the feature's mechanical description).

---

### Donation Platform

**Purpose on homepage (optional third spotlight, context-dependent):**
Speaks to the Secondary Audience — club owners and community managers. If the homepage needs to address this audience explicitly (and it should, given their size in the Roblox creator community), Donation Platform is the most relatable product for them.

**Value to communicate:**
Before: Accept donations through Sociabuzz/Saweria — platform doesn't know who the donor is in Roblox, can't automate in-game rewards, community members have to manually claim benefits.
After: Donation received → donor identity verified (Discord + Roblox) → in-game reward issued automatically.

**Visual strategy:**
Show the donor experience: Discord message notification → verified identity badge → automatic in-game reward. The visual follows the donor's journey, making the platform's connection to the real Roblox experience tangible.

**Conversion strategy:**
CTA: "Monetize your community" → Creator Panel.

---

### Asset Manager

**Purpose on homepage:**
Supporting product — best introduced in the Ecosystem section rather than given spotlight treatment. Its value is clear (organize your assets) but its before/after is less visceral than Audio Forge or License Manager. Feature on dedicated product page.

### Creator Panel

**Purpose on homepage:**
Creator Panel is not featured as a product spotlight — it is present throughout the homepage as the destination. It appears in the hero visual, as the context for other product spotlights, and as the final CTA destination. Spotlighting it as a product alongside others would reduce its status — it is the platform hub, and must be presented as such.

## Section 7 — Workflow Blueprint

### Purpose

The Workflow section educates visitors by showing what a creator's week looks like with Hibob Studio in place — not as an abstract description, but as a concrete sequence.

This section bridges the gap between "I understand what the products are" and "I understand how they fit into my actual work." A creator who can visualize themselves using the workflow will convert at a higher rate than a creator who understands the feature set but cannot see themselves using it.

---

### Narrative Structure

The Workflow section should tell the story of a specific creator type — the most relatable archetype for the primary audience — and show their week before and after Hibob Studio.

**Recommended archetype:** A Roblox developer who runs a club map, sells systems to other developers, and manages a Discord community. This creator touches Audio Forge (for club music), License Manager (for product sales), Donation Platform (for community support), Creator Identity (for verification), and Creator Panel (as their daily view). This archetype touches the maximum number of products in one believable story.

---

### Workflow Flow

**The creator's week — after Hibob Studio:**

```
MONDAY MORNING
Creator opens Creator Panel.
Status: 3 new license sales overnight → automatically whitelisted.
Pending: 2 audio tracks in Audio Forge queue → processing.
New: 5 donations received → in-game rewards issued automatically.
Action needed: 0.

AUDIO PUBLISHING
Opens Audio Forge.
Drops 8 new club tracks.
Batch process runs.
Asset IDs ready in 10 minutes.
Closes Audio Forge.

PRODUCT UPDATE
Pushes an update to their music system product.
License Manager notifies all existing license holders.
No manual contact required.

COMMUNITY MANAGEMENT
New member joins Discord.
Creator Identity verification confirms their Roblox account.
Discord role assigned automatically.
Creator doesn't see any of this — it happened without action.

FRIDAY REVIEW
Creator Panel shows weekly summary:
Revenue, active licenses, new community members, audio tracks live.
All from one view.
```

**The same creator's week — before Hibob Studio:**
The same activities, but each line becomes a manual task with a time cost. This is not shown in narrative form — it is shown as a contrast sidebar: "This used to take 3 hours. Now: 10 minutes."

---

### How this section educates visitors

The Workflow section does not list features — it shows consequences. The educational goal is not "creator knows what Audio Forge does" (the product spotlight achieves that). The educational goal is "creator can visualize Monday morning with Creator Panel open."

**The section works because:**

1. **It uses time as the currency.** Every creator understands time. Converting workflow improvements to time saved is universally legible.

2. **It shows the panel as the starting point.** The workflow begins with "Creator opens Creator Panel." This repetition trains the visitor's mental model: the panel is where the day starts.

3. **It makes automation visible.** The most powerful thing about platform infrastructure is what it does without asking. The workflow section must show the things that happened "while you were doing something else" — new licenses whitelisted, rewards issued, identity verified — because these are the moments that make a platform feel like infrastructure, not a tool.

---

### Visual Direction

**Option 1 — Timeline or daily log format:**
A visual representation of a creator's week, structured like a log or calendar. Each entry shows the action and the time cost (before) vs. the action and the time cost (after). The visual contrast between the two timelines makes the value proposition immediately scannable.

**Option 2 — Split screen with live panel view:**
Left side: the creator's workflow described in log form. Right side: the Creator Panel view that corresponds to each workflow moment. As the creator reads each step, the panel view updates to show what that step looks like in the interface.

**Option 3 — Before/after day comparison:**
Two columns. "Before Hibob Studio" (left): 8 manual tasks, 4+ hours. "After Hibob Studio" (right): 2 decisions made, 45 minutes. The visual compression of the "after" column is itself the argument.

## Section 8 — Social Proof Blueprint

### What trust signals should appear

Social proof on a platform homepage is most effective when it is specific, verifiable, and relevant to the audience's primary concern. Generic trust signals (star ratings, vague claim counts) provide minimal credibility with this audience.

**Trust signals ranked by impact:**

---

**Rank 1 — Working products (highest trust signal)**
The knowledge that Creator Panel exists and is accessible is more credible than any testimonial. The existence of a functional panel — visible in the hero visual and referenced throughout the homepage — communicates "this platform is real" more than any text can.

Implementation: Hero visual shows Creator Panel. Product spotlights reference the panel. The CTA leads directly to the panel. Every reference to the panel reinforces the signal.

---

**Rank 2 — Verified creator statistics**
Real numbers about real platform adoption. The format should be concrete and specific:

| Stat | What it communicates |
|------|---------------------|
| Active creators using platform | Scale of adoption |
| Licenses issued via License Manager | License Manager's real use |
| Audio tracks processed via Audio Forge | Audio Forge's real use |
| Community members verified via Creator Identity | Identity layer's real adoption |

**Important:** Statistics must be real. If the platform is early-stage, small real numbers are more credible than large invented numbers. "12 developers with active licenses" is more credible than "1000+ creators" if the 1000 isn't real. Update statistics as they grow.

**Presentation:** 3-4 stat cards, visually prominent but not dominant. Animated counter on scroll (this already exists in the current codebase and can be preserved).

---

**Rank 3 — Product testimonials from identifiable creators**
Testimonials from creators with verifiable Roblox or Discord presence. The credibility comes from verifiability — an anonymous testimonial with a first name is meaningless. A testimonial from a creator whose Roblox profile can be found and whose game has actual players is substantive.

**For each testimonial:**
- Creator name (real, not anonymized)
- Roblox username or Discord handle (verifiable)
- Which product they used
- Specific outcome or workflow change, not generic praise

**Example of weak testimonial (current state):**
"Satisfied with the results. Smooth communication and on-time delivery."
→ This describes a freelancer relationship, not a platform experience.

**Example of strong testimonial (target):**
"I used to spend Sunday evening uploading audio for the week. Audio Forge cut that to 15 minutes. I haven't manually uploaded a track in two months."
→ This describes a specific, verifiable workflow change.

---

**Rank 4 — Integration logos**
Discord + Roblox + Parcel logos shown as integration partners. These are recognizable trust anchors for this audience — they communicate that Hibob Studio operates within a known ecosystem and is not an isolated tool.

**Presentation:** A row of integration logos, labeled "Works with" or "Integrated with." Not a prominent section — a supporting visual element within or near the social proof section.

---

**Rank 5 — Creator community signal**
The Discord server — shown as an active community, not just a contact method — signals that other creators are using and discussing the platform. The Discord server member count and the visible activity level are trust signals for creators who are evaluating whether this platform has staying power.

**Presentation:** A Discord-style preview or community stat (e.g., "1,200 Roblox creators in the community") rather than just a "Join Discord" button.

---

**Rank 6 — Changelog / active development**
A reference to recent product updates — even a single line like "Latest: Audio Forge batch processing added — May 2026" — signals that the platform is being actively maintained. For a creator evaluating a platform commitment, evidence of continued development reduces perceived risk.

---

### What should NOT be in social proof

- Anonymous testimonials with no verifiable identity
- Commission-era testimonials about development quality (these reinforce old positioning)
- Generic "5-star rating" badges without context
- Fabricated statistics
- Social media follower counts (not relevant to platform credibility)

## Section 9 — Pricing Blueprint

### Position on page

Creator Plans appears as **Section 7 of 8** on the homepage — late in the journey, after value has been established through the Ecosystem, Product Spotlight, Workflow, and Social Proof sections.

**Why late:**
Pricing before value comprehension causes sticker shock. A creator who sees "Rp X per month" before understanding what they're getting will evaluate that number against zero context. The same number shown after the workflow section — after the creator has visualized their Monday morning with the platform in place — is evaluated against the time and friction it replaces.

**Why not at the very end:**
The final section is the closing CTA — one focused invitation to open the panel. Pricing cannot be the last thing before the CTA because it introduces a decision (which plan?) that competes with the conversion action (open the panel). Plans appear before the final CTA so the creator arrives at the closing CTA having already resolved the pricing question.

---

### Role in conversion

The Plans section does not need to convert visitors to paid subscribers on the homepage. Its role is to remove the pricing objection before the final CTA.

A creator who reaches the closing CTA without knowing the pricing will hesitate: "I want to try this, but what will it cost?" If the pricing section has already appeared, this objection is resolved.

**Secondary role:** The Plans section communicates that Hibob Studio has a structured, predictable model — not "everything is negotiable." This itself is a platform trust signal.

---

### Connection to Creator Plans

The pricing section on the homepage is a **summary**, not the full pricing page.

**What appears on the homepage:**
- 3 tier names and entry price points
- 2-3 key differentiators per tier (not a full feature matrix)
- The recommended/featured tier highlighted
- "See full comparison" link to `/plans`

**What appears on `/plans` only:**
- Full feature matrix
- FAQ
- Plan comparison table
- Upgrade/downgrade explanation

The homepage pricing section should be scannable in 15 seconds. A creator should be able to answer "which tier is probably right for me?" in 15 seconds and move on to the closing CTA. Detailed evaluation happens on `/plans`.

---

### Pricing framing

The Plans section frame must reflect platform access, not commission cost.

**Correct frame:** "Choose your level of platform access."
**Incorrect frame:** "Choose your commission tier." "Prices start from X." "Everything is negotiable."

Tier names should reflect creator progression:
- **Free / Starter:** Entry point — removes barriers, enables adoption
- **Creator:** Active creators who need full product access
- **Studio:** Teams and power users who need expanded capacity and multi-user features

## Section 10 — Panel Transition Blueprint

### The transition moment

The transition from `hibobstudio.com` to `panel.hibobstudio.com` is the most important conversion event on the homepage. Every element of the homepage is designed to make this transition feel natural, expected, and worth taking.

A transition that feels like a surprise ("oh, I'm leaving the website now?") is a conversion failure. A transition that feels like the logical next step ("of course, I need to open the panel to actually use this") is a conversion success.

---

### Expected mental model

By the time a creator clicks "Open Creator Panel," they should already have a mental model of what they are entering:

1. **The panel is a command center** — not a list of tools, not a settings page. It is where they will see their creator status, their connected products, and their operational overview.

2. **Creator Identity is the first step** — the homepage has communicated that Creator Identity is the foundation of the ecosystem. The creator arrives at the panel expecting to connect their Discord + Roblox identity before accessing full product functionality.

3. **The products they saw on the homepage are accessible from the panel** — Audio Forge, License Manager, Donation Platform. The products are not separate experiences — they are modules within the panel.

If the creator arrives at the panel with this mental model already formed, the onboarding process confirms expectations rather than surprises them. Expectation confirmation is the foundation of a positive first experience.

---

### Expectation setting on the homepage

The homepage sets expectations through multiple micro-signals throughout the page:

**In the hero:**
The panel visual in the hero shows the creator what the interface looks like. Not in detail — but enough to confirm "this is a real dashboard."

**In the ecosystem section:**
"All of this is accessible from your Creator Panel" — explicitly telling the creator that the panel is the hub for all products.

**In product spotlights:**
Every product CTA leads to the panel's product section — not to a separate install or a separate website. This establishes the pattern: products live in the panel.

**In the workflow section:**
The workflow begins with "Creator opens Creator Panel." Repeated exposure to this image builds the mental model.

**In the closing CTA:**
The closing CTA sets the final expectation before the click: "Your Creator Panel is where everything comes together. Connect your identity and access the full platform."

---

### The transition design

The button click should not feel like leaving the website. It should feel like opening a door.

**Technical note (for implementation):** The panel should open in the same tab (not a new tab) for desktop by default. Opening in a new tab creates the feeling of "departing" — like the website and the panel are different places. Same-tab navigation creates the feeling of "continuing" — like the panel is the next room.

**Panel landing experience** (note for panel design, not homepage design): The first thing a creator sees when they arrive at the panel from the homepage must confirm their decision. A landing page that shows "Loading..." or a blank dashboard with "No data yet" breaks the transition. The panel's first impression should be the Creator Identity setup — clear, purposeful, and immediately actionable.

### CTA principles

**Never use these labels on any homepage CTA:**
- "Hire Me" — commission-era, retired
- "Contact Us" — service-era, wrong frame
- "Get a Quote" — commission model
- "Discuss Project" — project-based model
- "Learn More" (without destination modifier) — passive, no intent signal

**CTA concentration:** No section should have more than two CTAs. Sections with more than two CTAs create decision paralysis — the visitor becomes uncertain which action to take and often takes none.

**CTA label principle:** The label should describe what happens after the click, not what the creator should feel about clicking. "Open Creator Panel" describes the result. "Get Started Today" describes an attitude. Label the result.

**CTA hierarchy:** On any section with two CTAs, visual hierarchy must be clear. Primary (solid filled, full color) vs. Secondary (outline or ghost, lighter). Never two equal-weight CTAs.

## Section 12 — Content Density

### What should be short

**Hero headline:** 4-8 words maximum. The headline is not an explanation — it is a declaration. Every word beyond 8 weakens the impact.

**Hero subheadline:** 1-2 sentences. Supporting context, not elaboration. A creator should be able to read it in one breath.

**Product names in ecosystem section:** Name + one-line functional description only. The ecosystem section is an overview, not a feature sheet. If a product requires 3 sentences to explain in the ecosystem view, the description is too long.

**CTA labels:** 2-4 words. Labels that require reading are labels that don't convert.

**Stats in social proof:** Stat + unit + short label. "847 active creators" not "Our platform currently serves 847 active creators across multiple product categories."

**Navigation items:** Single words or two-word phrases. "Products," "Plans," "Docs." Not "Our Products & Services," "Pricing & Plans."

---

### What should be detailed

**Problem section:** This section earns the right to be longer because it is about the creator's experience, not about the platform. Specific, detailed description of manual workflows creates resonance. Vague descriptions do not. 3-5 specific examples of manual overhead are more effective than 1 abstract statement.

**Product spotlight — the before/after:** The before workflow needs enough detail to be recognizable. If the before description is too vague, the creator cannot see themselves in it. "Audio publishing is manual" is too vague. "Upload track 1, wait for moderation, copy asset ID. Repeat for track 2. Repeat for track 3." is recognizable.

**Plans section — what's included:** Each plan tier should have 4-6 specific feature inclusions listed. Not a full feature matrix (that belongs on `/plans`) but enough specificity to differentiate the tiers.

**Creator Identity section:** The concept of linking Discord + Roblox identity may be unfamiliar to some visitors. This section warrants slightly more explanation than other product sections — because understanding it is prerequisite to understanding how the rest of the platform works.

---

### What should be visual

**Ecosystem architecture:** The relationship between products is spatial — it must be shown, not just described. A text-only list of 8 products communicates nothing about their relationships. A visual hierarchy communicates the architecture before a word is read.

**Workflow section:** The creator's day is a sequence — it should be shown as a sequence, not described as a paragraph. Timeline, flow diagram, or step representation.

**Product spotlights — before/after contrast:** The contrast between the old workflow and the new workflow is most legible as a visual comparison. Two columns, two states, immediately comparable.

**Integration logos:** The partnership with Discord, Roblox, and Parcel is most communicated by showing their logos — no text needed.

---

### What should be interactive (optional enhancements, not requirements)

**Counter animation for stats:** Already implemented in the codebase. Keep. Numbers that animate as they scroll into view draw attention and create a moment of engagement.

**Ecosystem hover states:** In the ecosystem diagram, hovering on a product could expand its one-line description or highlight its connection lines. Adds discovery without adding content.

**Plans toggle (if applicable):** If plans have annual vs. monthly pricing, a toggle between the two is standard interaction for pricing sections. Non-negotiable if two pricing periods exist.

**Workflow timeline scrubber:** A scrollable or tabbable representation of the creator's week — Monday through Friday — with the panel state shown for each day. Interactive versions of this are higher engagement but require implementation investment.

## Section 13 — Mobile Experience

### How the narrative survives mobile

The mobile homepage is not a compressed version of the desktop homepage — it is a resequenced version of the same narrative. The story must survive because the story is what converts, not the layout.

**What stays identical on mobile:**
- The section order (Hero → Problem → Ecosystem → Spotlight → Workflow → Social Proof → Plans → CTA)
- The messaging and copy
- The CTA hierarchy (primary always above secondary)
- The trust signals

**What changes on mobile:**

---

### Sections that simplify on mobile

**Hero:** Two-column desktop layout (text left, visual right) becomes single column. Text stack first (headline, subheadline, CTA), then platform visual below. The visual may be reduced in size or replaced with a simpler representation. Hero must remain impactful without relying on layout — headline and CTA carry the weight.

**Ecosystem visual:** The hierarchical ecosystem diagram is desktop-first. On mobile, the diagram simplifies to a vertical stack showing the same layer hierarchy. Layer labels remain. Connection lines may be simplified to single connecting line between layers. Product cards stack vertically with name and one-line description.

**Product spotlights:** Desktop uses a 50/50 split between description and visual. Mobile stacks: description first, visual below. Visual may be simplified to a single key frame rather than a full comparative view.

**Plans table:** Multi-column plan comparison on desktop becomes a tabbed or horizontally scrollable single-column view on mobile. Only one plan is visible at a time — swipe to see others. The recommended plan is shown by default.

**Workflow section:** Timeline or flow diagram compresses to vertical sequence on mobile. Each step in the workflow appears as a card in a vertical stack.

---

### What remains critical on mobile

**The hero headline and primary CTA must remain above the fold on every device.** If the hero CTA is not visible without scrolling on mobile, the primary conversion path is broken for the majority of mobile visitors.

**The "Open Creator Panel" CTA must appear in the mobile navigation.** Because mobile visitors may never reach the bottom of the page, the primary CTA must be accessible from the sticky header on mobile — not hidden behind a hamburger menu that requires two taps.

**The Problem section must not be cut or compressed beyond recognition.** The resonance this section creates is equally important on mobile — if anything, mobile visitors who are skimming benefit more from specific, scannable problem descriptions than from abstractions.

**Platform name and category must be readable at first glance.** Mobile font sizing must maintain the hierarchy: headline largest, subheadline second, body smallest. A mobile hero where the badge text "Creator Infrastructure Platform" is smaller than 12px has failed.

## Section 14 — Desktop Experience

### How the narrative should feel on large screens

The desktop experience of the homepage should feel like opening a serious tool for the first time — the kind of tool where the quality of the interface signals the quality of the system behind it.

**Spatial rhythm:** Each section should have generous whitespace — both above and below. A homepage that feels cramped communicates "this was built quickly." A homepage with breathing room communicates "this was built with intention." The dark matte background makes whitespace feel like depth rather than emptiness.

**Visual scale:** On large screens, the ecosystem diagram and product spotlight visuals should be able to fill their space with clarity — not stretch to fill space they weren't designed for. Design all full-width sections to a maximum content width (typically 1200-1440px) with proportional padding on wide screens.

**Typography scale:** Desktop headlines can be larger than what works on mobile. Large display type (72-100px for H1) on dark background with gradient text treatment creates visual impact consistent with platform-category presentation. This is the standard for infrastructure and developer tools brands.

**Two-column sections:** Hero, Product Spotlights, and Workflow Before/After are naturally suited to two-column layouts on desktop. The left-right split creates visual tension and directs reading flow. These sections should be designed for desktop-first and then adapted to mobile, not the reverse.

**Sticky navigation at full width:** On desktop, the navigation bar has more room. "Open Creator Panel" as a button with label + icon in the top-right is visible without being obtrusive. The full navigation label text ("Products," "Plans," "Docs," "About") is readable at all times.

**Scroll depth indicators:** The existing scroll progress bar in the header is a desktop-appropriate detail that communicates reading position. Keep.

## Section 15 — Design Direction

### Visual principles within current brand identity

The design language that Hibob Studio has already established — dark matte background, purple gradient accents, creator aesthetic — is appropriate for a platform company. It does not need to change. It needs to be applied more consistently to platform-company communication patterns.

The following principles guide all visual decisions without requiring a redesign:

---

**Principle 1 — Dark background is infrastructure, not decoration.**

The dark matte background (`#03010f` or equivalent) communicates depth, seriousness, and technical credibility. It is the correct environment for a creator tool — it reads like a terminal, like a dashboard, like a professional application. This is not a coincidence. Builder-oriented brands — Vercel, Linear, Figma, Supabase — all use dark-first design because it signals "this was built for people who do serious work."

Application: Maintain dark matte as the primary background. Introduce lighter sections (slightly elevated dark, not white) sparingly to create section contrast without breaking the atmosphere.

---

**Principle 2 — Purple gradient is emphasis, not decoration.**

The purple gradient (`#a855f7` → `#7c3aed` → `#38bdf8`) is the most expressive element of the brand. When it appears everywhere, it loses meaning. When it appears at specific moments, it creates emphasis.

Application: Reserve gradient treatment for:
- The primary headline (key words in the H1)
- Primary CTA buttons
- Product names in ecosystem visualization
- Active state indicators in navigation
- Section labels / eyebrow text above headlines

Do not apply gradient to body text, secondary elements, or decorative backgrounds as a default. The gradient is signal — use it only where signal is needed.

---

**Principle 3 — Typography carries authority.**

Plus Jakarta Sans (already in the codebase) is the correct typeface for this brand — it reads as modern, technical, and clean without being sterile. Its character at large display sizes (the headline) and at small sizes (the body) is consistent.

Application:
- H1 headline: Maximum weight (900), largest size available, gradient treatment on key phrase
- H2 section headlines: Heavy weight (700-800), no gradient unless specifically indicated
- Section eyebrow labels: Medium weight (500), small, uppercase or small-caps, muted color
- Body copy: Regular weight (400), 16-18px, high contrast on dark background
- CTA labels: Semibold (600), button treatment

No italics in primary copywriting. No decorative font variants. Consistency of type weight communicates brand coherence.

---

**Principle 4 — Visual hierarchy is architecture.**

Every visual on the homepage — the ecosystem diagram, the product spotlights, the workflow diagram — should be structured to communicate hierarchy through visual organization. This means:

- Larger elements are more important
- Brighter/more saturated elements are active/available
- Muted/grayscale elements are secondary or Coming Soon
- Connected elements are visually connected (lines, shared containers, aligned axes)

Do not use decoration for visual interest. Use structure for visual meaning.

---

**Principle 5 — Animation serves information, not aesthetics.**

The existing scroll reveal animations (opacity + translate + blur) are appropriate — they guide reading attention without distracting from content. The floating animation on the hero character should be replaced when the hero character is replaced with a panel visual — the panel should not float, it should be stable, grounded, precise.

New animations should follow one rule: does this animation communicate something, or does it just move? The counter animation on stats communicates "this number is growing." A rotating gradient on a card communicates nothing — it is decoration.

Application:
- Keep scroll reveal animations
- Keep counter animations for stats
- Remove floating animations from platform interface visuals
- Add hover states to ecosystem diagram elements (informational, not decorative)
- Avoid parallax, particle effects, and auto-playing video unless they serve a specific informational purpose

## Section 16 — Remove Old Portfolio Thinking

The portfolio mindset is not just a set of specific elements — it is a frame that shapes how every decision gets made. Below are the specific expressions of portfolio thinking that the new homepage must actively avoid.

---

### Portfolio pattern: First-person personal identity

**What it looks like:** "Hi, I'm HibobTheDev." / "I Build Roblox Experiences." / "Why Me?" / "Games I've Built"

**Why it exists:** Portfolio sites are built to establish the credibility of an individual. The individual is the product. First-person language is how individuals introduce themselves.

**How the new homepage avoids it:** The homepage speaks as a platform, not a person. "Hibob Studio" is the subject of every sentence, not "I" or "we" in personal context. Section labels describe platform capability, not personal achievement. The brand name displayed everywhere is "Hibob Studio" — not the founder's handle.

---

### Portfolio pattern: "Hire Me" as the primary action

**What it looks like:** "Hire Me" in the header, in the hero, in the mobile menu. The CTA that proposes a client-contractor relationship.

**Why it exists:** The primary conversion action for a freelancer is contact — "tell me about your project." "Hire Me" is the action that begins that conversation.

**How the new homepage avoids it:** Zero instances of "Hire Me" anywhere on the page. The primary action is "Open Creator Panel" — which proposes a creator-platform relationship. The entire CTA vocabulary is about access, not contact.

---

### Portfolio pattern: Commission pricing as the pricing model

**What it looks like:** "Commission Pricing." Tier names: "GUI Design / Scripting / Full Game Dev." "Everything is negotiable."

**Why it exists:** Freelancers charge for time and deliverables. The pricing model reflects this — each tier is a different scope of work for hire.

**How the new homepage avoids it:** Plans are structured around platform access, not work scope. Tier names reflect creator progression, not service type. "Everything is negotiable" is removed — platform pricing is published and fixed. The Plans section language frames cost as subscription access to infrastructure, not payment for labor.

---

### Portfolio pattern: Portfolio gallery as credibility signal

**What it looks like:** "Games I've Built" — 9 project cards, each linking to a Roblox game, each with "Play Now" CTA.

**Why it exists:** For a developer for hire, past work is the primary credibility signal. "I built this game" answers "can you build my game?"

**How the new homepage avoids it:** The homepage does not include a portfolio gallery. Past work as a credibility signal is appropriate for the About page — it establishes the expertise behind the platform. On the homepage, credibility is established by platform statistics, working products, and creator testimonials — not by the founder's past projects. The question the new homepage answers is not "can you build my game?" but "does this platform work?" Those are answered differently.

---

### Portfolio pattern: Commission services menu

**What it looks like:** "Services: World Building, Luau Scripting, GUI Design"

**Why it exists:** A freelancer's homepage needs to communicate what services are offered for hire.

**How the new homepage avoids it:** No services section. No menu of things the company does for clients. Platform capabilities are described through product descriptions — what the products enable the creator to do, not what Hibob Studio will do for the creator. The grammatical subject of every product description is the creator, not Hibob Studio.

---

### Portfolio pattern: Contact as conversion

**What it looks like:** "Open for Commission" / "Have a Project? Let's Work Together" / Discord and Email as primary CTAs.

**Why it exists:** For a freelancer, the conversion is making contact. The project begins with a conversation.

**How the new homepage avoids it:** The conversion is a panel session, not a conversation. Discord and Email are available as support channels and community (in the footer and in the community section) but they are not the primary or secondary CTA anywhere on the homepage. "Let's work together" language is replaced by "access the platform" language.

---

### Portfolio pattern: Personal pronouns in testimonials

**What it looks like:** "Hibob's work is clean." / "Satisfied with the results." (These describe a person's work product, not a platform's impact.)

**Why it exists:** Commission clients evaluate the freelancer — their work quality, their communication, their reliability.

**How the new homepage avoids it:** Testimonials describe platform impact, not developer quality. "I haven't manually uploaded a track in two months" describes a platform benefit. "The work was clean and systematic" describes a contractor's output. The new testimonials are about what changed in the creator's workflow — not about how good the developer was to work with.

## Section 18 — Final Recommendation

### If a visitor only scrolls once through the homepage: what is the single message they should remember?

**"Hibob Studio is the platform that serious Roblox creators use to run their workflow professionally — not a freelancer, not a tool, a platform."**

This is the message the homepage is designed to deliver. Not a product name. Not a feature. Not a price. A positioning claim that the creator carries with them after they leave — whether they converted that session or not.

The creator who leaves the homepage with this message will return when they are ready. The creator who leaves without this message may return without knowing why — or may not return at all.

---

### What this means in practice

Every design decision, every copy choice, every CTA label, every visual direction in this document is subordinated to the delivery of this single message.

When a copywriter is uncertain between two headline options: choose the one that more clearly communicates "platform for serious Roblox creators."

When a designer is uncertain between two visual treatments: choose the one that makes this look more like a platform and less like a personal project.

When an implementer is uncertain between two interaction patterns: choose the one that makes the transition to Creator Panel feel more like continuing a journey and less like leaving a website.

The homepage has one job. This document defines how to do that job. Everything else is execution.
